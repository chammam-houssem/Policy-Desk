## Evolution of the electricity mix since 2000

### Structural pattern: тАЬgasificationтАЭ of power rather than diversification
From ~2000 onward, Algeria expanded electricity supply primarily through gas-fired technologiesтАФopenтАСcycle gas turbines and combinedтАСcycle gas turbinesтАФreflecting abundant domestic gas, the need for rapid capacity additions, and (in some analyses) local constraints such as water requirements that can favor gas turbine configurations over steam-heavy alternatives. юИАciteюИВturn8search3юИВturn8search13юИВturn8search19юИБ

### Role of coal, gas, oil, hydro, solar, wind
Coal has no material role in AlgeriaтАЩs electricity generation mix (and coal-fired power is effectively absent); where coal appears in national energy statistics it is in overall energy supply at very small shares, not as a major power fuel. юИАciteюИВturn32search10юИВturn33view2юИБ

### Turning points and why they matter
A key policy turning point was the 2011 renewable energy and energy efficiency plan (and its later updates), which reframed renewables from a niche R&D effort into a national capacity program with an export option and explicit electricity-generation share targets. юИАciteюИВturn6view1юИВturn6view2юИБ

## Analytical synthesis

### Is AlgeriaтАЩs transition driven more by decarbonization or energy security?
The balance of evidence points to **energy security and economic rent preservation** as the dominant driver. The clearest indicator is that official renewable plans explicitly emphasize gas savings, which is fundamentally an energyтАСeconomy logic: reducing domestic gas burn protects export capacity and fiscal revenues. юИАciteюИВturn6view0юИВturn3search9юИВturn8search19юИБ

Decarbonization is still presentтАФAlgeriaтАЩs NDC includes a renewable electricity generation share targetтАФbut it is framed within conditionality and sits within a system whose near-term operational needs (meeting rising load reliably) are satisfied most cheaply and flexibly by gas given legacy infrastructure. юИАciteюИВturn6view2юИВturn32search12юИВturn5view0юИБ

### Why does fossil generation remain dominant despite renewable expansion?
Fossil dominance persists because:

The installed base and dispatch logic overwhelmingly favor gas. In 2023, non-renewable capacity (~28 GW) dwarfed renewable (~0.6 GW), and generation shares mirror this. юИАciteюИВturn0search11юИВturn5view0юИВturn32search12юИБ

Gas plants provide system services (inertia, peaking, balancing) that must be replaced or complemented as variable renewables scale. With limited hydro potential and minimal storage penetration so far, gas remains the default balancing resource. юИАciteюИВturn5view0юИВturn3search21юИВturn32search12юИБ

### What explains the gap between targets and actual deployment?
The gap is best explained by the interaction of **ambitious capacity targets** with **slow procurement execution and system bottlenecks**:

Targets have shifted over time (22 GW by 2030 vs later re-scaling to 16 GW by 2035 in some narratives), signaling planning volatility and reducing investor certainty. юИАciteюИВturn6view0юИВturn30search11юИВturn30search7юИВturn30search21юИБ

Tenderization is necessary but not sufficient; it requires bankable PPAs, credible offtake, clear land and grid connection processes, and financing pathways. Institutional embedding inside state entities can create a тАЬsingle-channelтАЭ execution model: effective when mobilized, but vulnerable to administrative delays and capacity constraints when multiple gigawatts must be delivered rapidly. юИАciteюИВturn30search0юИВturn5view3юИВturn30search16юИБ
