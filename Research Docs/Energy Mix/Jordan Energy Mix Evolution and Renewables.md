# Jordan's electricity transition: security-driven, structurally constrained

**Jordan has built one of the Middle East's most impressive renewable energy sectors — reaching ~27% of electricity generation by 2024 from near zero in 2014 — yet its transition is fundamentally shaped by energy security imperatives rather than decarbonization ambitions, and is now constrained by the very contracts designed to ensure that security.** The country's electricity system has undergone three structural shifts since 2000: from oil dependence to Egyptian gas, from Egyptian gas to Israeli gas and LNG after the Arab Spring pipeline attacks, and from fossil monopoly to a hybrid fossil-renewable system. Each shift was crisis-driven. Today, Jordan faces a paradox: take-or-pay gas and oil shale contracts worth billions lock in fossil generation even as solar costs have fallen 83%, renewable capacity approaches **2,700 MW**, and the government has raised its 2030 target from 31% to 50% of electricity from renewables. Understanding this tension between ambition and structural lock-in is essential to evaluating Jordan's energy trajectory.

---

## Three structural shifts reshaped Jordan's electricity system since 2000

Jordan's electricity generation grew roughly **3.4 times** between 2000 and 2024 — from 6,935 GWh to approximately 23,675 GWh — driven by population growth (including over 1 million Syrian refugees after 2011), urbanisation, and rising cooling demand. The system's fuel composition, however, changed dramatically across three distinct phases.

**Phase 1 — Oil dominance (2000–2007).** At the turn of the millennium, Jordan generated virtually all its electricity from imported crude oil and heavy fuel oil. The country depended on preferential oil supplies from Iraq under the UN Oil-for-Food programme. When the 2003 Iraq invasion ended this arrangement, Jordan pivoted to the newly completed Arab Gas Pipeline from Egypt. Natural gas rapidly displaced oil, reaching **60–70%** of generation by 2008–2010.

**Phase 2 — Gas crisis and emergency diversification (2011–2015).** The Arab Spring upended Jordan's fragile energy equilibrium. Insurgents in the Sinai Peninsula attacked the Egyptian gas pipeline **14 times** between 2011 and 2014, eventually halting flows entirely. Jordan was forced back onto expensive diesel and heavy fuel oil, triggering a fiscal catastrophe: NEPCO (the state transmission company and single buyer) accumulated **$5.4 billion in operating losses** between 2011 and 2014. Public debt surged from under 60% of GDP to over 80%. The crisis drove three simultaneous responses: construction of a floating LNG regasification terminal at Aqaba (operational 2014), signing of a controversial **$10 billion, 15-year gas contract** with Israel's Leviathan field, and passage of the 2012 Renewable Energy and Energy Efficiency Law that launched utility-scale renewables.

**Phase 3 — Fossil-renewable hybrid (2016–present).** Israeli gas deliveries began in January 2020, restoring stable supply. Meanwhile, renewables surged from effectively zero in 2014 to **~24–27% of generation by 2024**. But a new element emerged: the **470 MW Attarat oil shale power plant** came online in 2022 under a punitive take-or-pay PPA at **$0.17/kWh**, suddenly contributing ~13–15% of generation. Jordan's current system thus runs on three pillars: Israeli/LNG gas (~58%), renewables (~27%), and oil shale (~15%), with residual oil at 3–4%.

## The current mix reveals a critical capacity-generation mismatch

The most recent complete data (2023–2024) shows Jordan's electricity system has significant overcapacity and a revealing gap between installed capacity shares and actual generation shares.

**Generation breakdown (2023, IEA/WNA data):** Natural gas produced **62%** of electricity (14.7 TWh), solar contributed **15%** (3.6 TWh), oil shale/coal added **13%** (3.0 TWh), wind generated **7%** (1.6 TWh), and oil/petroleum accounted for the remaining **4%**. By end-2024, natural gas had declined to approximately **58%** while renewables rose to ~27% and oil shale reached ~15%.

**Capacity breakdown (end-2024, ~7,438 MW total):** Natural gas holds roughly **40%** of installed capacity (~3,000 MW), solar PV accounts for **~27%** (~2,000 MW including distributed), wind represents **~8%** (~620 MW), oil shale sits at **~6%** (470 MW), and legacy oil/diesel plants retain **~12%** (~900 MW). Hydropower is negligible at ~12 MW.

**The mismatch matters analytically.** Renewables hold approximately **37% of installed capacity** but produce only **~24% of electricity**. Gas holds ~40% of capacity but generates ~58–62%. Oil shale, with just 6% of capacity, produces 13–15% of electricity because it runs as baseload under its take-or-pay contract. This gap reflects three factors: lower capacity factors for solar (~18–22%) and wind (~30–38%) compared to gas plants running at high utilisation; curtailment of renewable output due to grid constraints; and contractual obligations that keep fossil plants running regardless of renewable availability. Total installed capacity (~7.4 GW) vastly exceeds peak demand (~4 GW), creating a **~45% capacity surplus** — a structural overcapacity problem with significant financial consequences.

## Solar became Jordan's renewable success story through three procurement rounds

Solar PV dominates Jordan's renewable portfolio and exemplifies one of the most dramatic cost declines anywhere in the MENA region.

**Round 1 (2012–2016)** used a feed-in tariff mechanism with government-published ceiling prices. Developers submitted proposals for projects primarily in the Ma'an Development Area. The benchmark project — **Shams Ma'an (52.5 MW, First Solar)** — secured a tariff of **$0.148/kWh**. Twelve solar consortia were awarded contracts, with most projects commissioned between June and October 2016 in a concentrated burst of construction. The first utility-scale plant, Al-Badiya Philadelphia (13 MW), connected in October 2015.

**Round 2 (2013–2019)** shifted to competitive bidding and moved north to the Mafraq area. Four 50 MW projects were awarded at dramatically lower tariffs: the lowest at **$0.0613/kWh** — roughly 60% cheaper than Round 1. This cost decline, achieved in just two years, demonstrated that Jordan's solar resource (5–7 kWh/m²/day, 300+ sunny days) combined with competitive procurement could deliver world-class prices. FRV, ACWA Power, and other international developers commissioned their projects between late 2018 and mid-2019.

**Round 3 (2018+)** pushed costs further, achieving a record-low bid of **$0.02488/kWh** — an 83% decline from Round 1. At this price, new solar undercuts gas generation by a wide margin (gas-fired electricity costs roughly $0.06–0.08/kWh depending on fuel prices).

**Parallel to the competitive rounds**, government-owned projects funded by foreign grants added significant capacity. The **Quweira Solar Plant (103 MW DC)**, funded by Abu Dhabi's ADFD, came online in April 2018. The **Baynouna Solar Park (200 MW)**, developed by Masdar at a cost of ~$260 million, became Jordan's largest single solar project when commissioned in December 2020, generating 563 GWh annually.

**Distributed solar exploded** through net metering regulations. By end-2024, **1,082 MW** of distributed solar was installed across 74,145 projects — exceeding utility-scale solar capacity (992 MW). This growth, however, created financial stress: commercial and industrial customers self-generating via rooftop solar eroded NEPCO's revenue base, prompting the government to discontinue net metering for new installations in September 2024, replacing it with a less generous net billing framework.

**CSP never materialised in Jordan** despite featuring prominently in the 2007 energy strategy (which envisioned 300–600 MW). The combination of PV's dramatic cost decline, Jordan's severe water scarcity (CSP requires cooling water), faster PV deployment timelines (12–18 months versus 3–5 years), and simpler financing meant that no commercial CSP plant was ever built. Only a small research facility exists at the National Energy Research Centre.

## Wind development plateaued after a strong initial build-out

Jordan's wind sector achieved early distinction: the **117 MW Tafila Wind Farm**, commissioned in September 2015, was the first commercial utility-scale wind farm in the Middle East. Developed by a Masdar-led consortium at a cost of $287 million, its 38 Vestas turbines generate approximately 390 GWh annually, implying a capacity factor of **~38%** — excellent by global standards and reflecting the strong wind resources (7–8.5 m/s) in southern Jordan's highlands.

Between 2015 and 2021, eight additional wind farms were commissioned, bringing total installed wind capacity to approximately **622 MW**. Key projects include the government-owned Ma'an Wind Farm (80 MW, 2016), Al-Rajef (86 MW, 2018), Fujeij (89 MW, KEPCO, 2019), Mass Energy (100 MW, 2020), and Daehan (51.75 MW, 2021). Wind generation is geographically concentrated in two governorates: **Tafileh** (~321 MW) and **Ma'an** (~300 MW), where the terrain creates favourable wind corridors.

**No new wind farms have been commissioned since July 2021.** The 2019 freeze on new renewable projects over 1 MW, combined with grid absorption concerns and NEPCO's financial difficulties, halted the pipeline. Wind contributed approximately **7% of Jordan's electricity in 2023** (1.6 TWh), and curtailment runs at roughly 17% of wind capacity due to low-demand periods coinciding with peak generation. The freeze was lifted in September 2024, but no new wind projects had reached commissioning by early 2026.

## Hydropower is structurally negligible, but pumped storage could transform the system

Jordan's total installed hydropower capacity amounts to approximately **10–12 MW**, almost entirely from a small installation at King Talal Dam (6 MW). Annual hydro generation is typically below 20–30 GWh — less than **0.1%** of total electricity. This is not a policy failure but a geographic reality: Jordan is one of the world's most water-scarce countries, with less than 100 m³ of renewable freshwater per capita annually. All thirteen major dams prioritise irrigation and municipal water supply over power generation; several operate at only 20–30% of reservoir capacity.

**Pumped hydro storage, however, could be transformational.** The flagship **Al-Mujib Pumped Storage Project** would deliver **450 MW / 3,150 MWh** (seven hours at full capacity) by constructing an upper reservoir (Wadi Nukheila Dam) connected to the existing Mujib Dam via a 511-metre head differential. A full feasibility study was completed in September 2022 with GIZ funding, and the estimated cost is **~$650 million**. The project is listed in Jordan's Economic Modernisation Vision PPP portfolio, and the World Bank has indicated it could "double renewable energy capacity by 2035" by resolving the storage bottleneck. However, the EU's Mitigation Action Facility lists the project as **"Inactive"**, suggesting persistent financing challenges. No utility-scale battery storage has been confirmed operational either — only approximately 50 MW exists, a fraction of what is needed to manage 2,700 MW of intermittent renewables.

The absence of meaningful storage is arguably Jordan's single greatest constraint on further renewable integration. Without it, every additional MW of solar deepens the "duck curve" problem — daytime surplus, evening deficit — forcing continued reliance on gas for dispatchable power.

## Policy targets have escalated faster than deployment capacity

Jordan's renewable energy policy has evolved through four strategic documents, each more ambitious than the last, though implementation has consistently lagged aspiration.

The **2007 National Energy Strategy** set an initial target of 10% renewable energy in the total energy mix by 2020, with 1,800 MW of combined solar and wind capacity. This target was broadly met: by 2020, approximately 2 GW of renewables were installed and contributed ~19% of electricity generation. The **2020–2030 Energy Strategy**, approved in July 2020, set a target of **31% renewables in electricity generation by 2030** (corresponding to 3,200 MW installed capacity), alongside 14% of total primary energy from renewables. Natural gas was projected to decline from 61% to 53% of generation.

Then, in 2023–2024, multiple senior officials — including Energy Minister Saleh Kharabsheh and EMRC Chairman Ziad Saideh — announced a revised target of **50% of electricity from renewables by 2030**. This target has not yet been formalised in a published strategy document (the comprehensive strategy revision is reportedly underway), but it is now cited as the de facto government position.

**Jordan's Updated NDC (2021)** commits to a **31% GHG reduction** below business-as-usual by 2030, but critically, **84% of this target is conditional on international financing** (26% conditional versus 5% unconditional). The September 2025 "NDC 3.0 Vision" document indicates that by 2024, Jordan had achieved approximately 24.8% of the 31% reduction target. The document references embedding a "2050 net-zero trajectory" but does not constitute a formal net-zero commitment.

**Nuclear energy** has followed a separate, often contradictory track. The 2020 Energy Strategy explicitly excluded nuclear, yet the Jordan Atomic Energy Commission continues pursuing Small Modular Reactor feasibility studies, with a target of selecting a vendor for operation "by 2030" — primarily for electricity and seawater desalination.

A critical analytical point: the 50% target for 2030 would require adding roughly **2,000–3,000 MW** of new renewable capacity in approximately four years, given current generation patterns. Between 2019 and 2024, however, only about 600 MW of new renewable capacity was added (mostly distributed solar), because the auction freeze halted utility-scale procurement for five years. Closing this gap requires an unprecedented acceleration that current grid infrastructure, storage capacity, and institutional coordination cannot yet support.

## The future trajectory hinges on interconnections, hydrogen, and contract management

Jordan's forward-looking energy plans extend beyond domestic renewables into three strategic domains.

**Regional interconnections** are central to absorbing surplus generation and improving energy security. The existing Egypt link (500 MW) is being upgraded to **2,000 MW** (agreement signed August 2025). A Jordan-Iraq interconnection became operational in March 2024 at 40 MW, with expansion to 150–200 MW expected by mid-2025. The most significant planned link is with **Saudi Arabia** — a 400 kV, 500 MW (Phase 1) to 1,000 MW (Phase 2) connection, with commercial operations targeted for end-2029. Jordan explicitly positions itself as a regional electricity trading hub connecting the Mashreq, Gulf, and potentially North Africa.

**Green hydrogen** represents Jordan's most ambitious industrial policy bet. The draft National Green Hydrogen Strategy (2023, cabinet-approved 2025) targets **0.6 million tonnes** of green hydrogen production annually by 2030, with 85% destined for export as ammonia via Aqaba. This would require approximately **8 GW of dedicated renewable capacity** for electrolysis — more than double Jordan's current total renewable base. Early projects include the Jordan Green Ammonia plant in Aqaba (100,000–300,000 tonnes/year, ~€1.2 billion) and a Chinese-backed UEG project ($1.15 billion). These targets are highly aspirational given that current levelised hydrogen costs in Jordan ($6.50–7.60/kg) far exceed the 2050 target of $2–3/kg, and no operational production facilities exist.

**The Aqaba-Amman National Water Carrier** — a €4 billion desalination and conveyance megaproject — will include a **281–310 MWp solar farm** at Quweira and represents the convergence of Jordan's water and energy crises. Construction began in early 2025 under a Meridiam-SUEZ consortium, with completion targeted for 2028–2029.

## Energy security, not climate, drives Jordan's transition

The analytical evidence strongly indicates that **energy security is the primary driver** of Jordan's electricity transition, with decarbonisation functioning as a co-benefit rather than a motivating force. Every major policy inflection — the 2012 Renewable Energy Law, the LNG terminal, the Israeli gas deal, the renewable procurement rounds — was triggered by supply disruptions, not climate commitments. Jordan's NDC is 84% conditional on foreign financing. Its Green Hydrogen Strategy is oriented toward export revenue. Its 50% renewable target serves the dual purpose of reducing gas import bills and managing geopolitical risk.

The June 2025 Israeli gas supply disruption — a 33-day halt during the Iran-Israel conflict, followed by another suspension in early 2026 — starkly illustrated the persistence of energy vulnerability. Jordan has arguably substituted **Egypt-dependence for Israel-dependence**, with Israeli gas now supplying over 50% of the country's gas needs under a contract that carries a $1.5 billion cancellation penalty.

**Why fossil generation persists despite cheap renewables** is explained by the **take-or-pay contract trap**. The $10 billion Israeli gas agreement requires payment for 300 MMcf/day regardless of consumption. The Attarat oil shale PPA locks in $0.17/kWh — nearly seven times the latest solar auction price — for the plant's lifetime. NEPCO must honour these contracts: every new MW of solar that displaces gas does not proportionally reduce total system costs because NEPCO still pays for the unburned gas. This creates a **perverse disincentive** against renewable expansion. The 2019–2024 auction freeze was a direct response to this overcapacity paradox. NEPCO's accumulated debt of approximately **$7.8 billion** — representing ~18% of national debt — is both a consequence of past fuel crises and a constraint on future investment.

The gap between Jordan's 50% target and reality is therefore not primarily a technical or resource problem. Jordan has excellent solar irradiance, strong wind resources, and proven developer interest at world-class prices. The binding constraints are **structural**: contractual lock-in with fossil fuels, insufficient grid and storage infrastructure, NEPCO's financial fragility, and institutional coordination challenges between MEMR, NEPCO, and EMRC. Resolution requires either renegotiating gas contracts (difficult given penalty clauses), substantially expanding interconnections to export surplus power, deploying grid-scale storage (the 450 MW Mujib project remains unfunded), or simply waiting for contracts to expire (the Leviathan deal runs until ~2035). Jordan's transition is real, but it is a transition operating within binding structural constraints that no amount of target-raising can override without addressing the underlying institutional and contractual architecture.
