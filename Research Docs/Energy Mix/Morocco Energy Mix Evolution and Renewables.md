# **The Strategic Decarbonization of the Moroccan Power Sector: A Systemic Analysis of Policy Evolution, Grid Integration, and Renewable Energy Trajectories**

The structural evolution of the Moroccan electricity sector from the beginning of the twenty-first century to the 2026 horizon represents a paradigmatic shift in energy governance within the Middle East and North Africa (MENA) region. As a nation historically devoid of substantial indigenous hydrocarbon reserves, Morocco has transitioned from a model of absolute energy dependency to a diversified, renewable-centric strategy aimed at achieving energy sovereignty and industrial competitiveness.1 This transformation was formalized through the 2009 National Energy Strategy, which established a comprehensive roadmap for the deployment of solar, wind, and hydroelectric resources. By late 2024, Morocco’s installed electricity capacity had reached approximately 12.28 GW, with renewable sources—including conventional hydropower and pumped storage—accounting for 44.3% to 45.3% of this total, a significant increase from the negligible shares seen two decades prior.3

## **The Macroeconomic Imperative and the 2009 Strategic Pivot**

The fundamental driver of Moroccan energy policy is the kingdom’s status as a net energy importer, which historically exposed the national economy to the volatility of global commodity markets. In the early 2000s, Morocco relied on imports for approximately 96% of its energy needs.2 The fiscal pressure of this dependency became particularly acute during the oil price spikes of 2005 and 2008, where import costs surged to $8 billion annually, contributing to significant budget deficits and weighing heavily on the national trade balance.2 In 2012, energy subsidies alone accounted for nearly 6.6% of Morocco’s GDP, highlighting the unsustainable nature of a fossil-fuel-dependent model for a growing emerging economy.2

Against this backdrop, the 2009 National Energy Strategy was launched under the guidance of King Mohammed VI. The strategy was not merely an environmental commitment but a core macroeconomic instrument designed to reduce the trade deficit, ensure security of supply, and foster a local industrial base for clean technologies.1 The initial goal was to reach a 42% share of renewable energy in the total installed capacity by 2020, a target that was subsequently increased to 52% by 2030 and an ambitious 80% by 2050\.9

The correlation between economic growth and electricity demand has remained strong throughout this period. Between 2004 and 2018, total primary energy consumption rose at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 5%.6 By 2023, per-capita electricity consumption had increased by 15% relative to 2000 levels, reaching 0.997 MWh per capita.12 This growth is largely underpinned by the residential and industrial sectors, which together account for over 70% of final electricity demand.12

## **The Thermal Foundation: Coal, Gas, and the "Bridge" Strategy**

Despite the aggressive expansion of renewables, Morocco’s baseload power remains firmly anchored in thermal generation, specifically coal. In 2023 and 2024, coal-fired plants continued to dominate the electricity mix, accounting for approximately 60% to 62.1% of total generation.3 Facilities such as the Jorf Lasfar complex, with its 2.05 GW storage park and extended power purchase agreements (PPAs) running through 2044, represent a significant legacy infrastructure that provides the system with stability and relatively low-cost energy compared to imported oil products.6

The reliance on coal presents a decarbonization dilemma. While Morocco endorsed parts of the coal exit pledge at COP26, committing to cease building new plants, it has not yet committed to a complete phase-out by 2040\.9 The systemic importance of coal for grid frequency control and baseload supply means that any transition requires a massive and reliable replacement capacity, a role that was originally envisioned for natural gas.

### **The Natural Gas Roadmap and Recent Infrastructure Delays**

Natural gas was intended to serve as a transition fuel to displace coal and supplement variable renewables. However, the termination of the Maghreb-Europe Gas (MEG) pipeline transit from Algeria in late 2021 forced a major strategic pivot.14 Morocco quickly adapted by reversing the MEG pipeline flow to import gas from Spanish LNG terminals.14

The 2024-2030 Natural Gas Roadmap planned for the construction of several LNG import terminals to increase gas consumption from 1 bcm to 8 bcm by 2027\.16 However, in February 2026, the Ministry of Energy Transition suspended tenders for a $1 billion LNG terminal at the Nador West Med port.14 This decision, characterized as an indefinite pause, reflects a need to reassess project assumptions in the face of global gas market volatility and concerns over monopolistic pricing risks.15 Similarly, the Tendrara gas project in eastern Morocco has faced delays, with commercial production now expected in the third quarter of 2026 due to setbacks in micro-LNG facility equipment delivery.18 These infrastructure bottlenecks suggest that while gas remains a critical system stabilizer, its role as a "bridge" fuel is currently constrained by logistical and geopolitical complexities.

## **The Solar Trajectory: From Concentrated Thermal to PV and Storage**

Morocco’s solar energy program, managed by the Moroccan Agency for Sustainable Energy (MASEN), is perhaps the most visible element of its transition. The program began with the Noor Ouarzazate complex, a flagship installation that utilized Concentrated Solar Power (CSP) technology to provide thermal storage and dispatchable solar energy after sunset.8

### **The Technological Evolution at Noor Ouarzazate and Midelt**

The early focus on CSP was driven by the need for storage at a time when battery costs were prohibitively high. Noor I, II, and III provided a combined 510 MW of CSP capacity with storage ranging from three to seven hours.19 However, the high capital expenditure and operational complexity of CSP have led to a significant policy shift toward Solar Photovoltaic (PV) combined with Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS).5

In 2024, the Noor Ouarzazate plant suffered from repeated technical faults and storage failures, leading to nine months of downtime and $47 million in financial losses.20 This experience has informed the design of the next generation of projects, specifically the Noor Midelt complex. While Noor Midelt I was originally designed as a hybrid CSP-PV project, subsequent tenders for Noor Midelt II and III (400 MW each) have pivoted toward PV-plus-battery configurations.20

## **Wind Power: Consistent Growth and High Capacity Factors**

Wind energy has emerged as the most cost-effective and reliable renewable resource in the Moroccan mix, often achieving capacity factors that rival conventional generation in specific coastal corridors.24 By 2024, wind capacity reached approximately 2,400 MW, with major installations centered in the wind-rich regions of the north (Tanger-Tetouan) and the south (Guelmim-Oued Noun and the southern provinces).4

The Jbel Lahdid wind farm, a 270 MW project commissioned in 2024, exemplifies the ongoing expansion of the wind fleet.4 Furthermore, the repowering of older sites, such as the Koudia Al Baida farm in Tetouan, demonstrates a mature phase of development where older turbines are replaced with larger, more efficient units to maximize site potential.4 The wind resource in Morocco is particularly valuable due to its daily consistency; prevailing winds often blow most strongly in the late afternoon and evening, aligning well with the national demand peak and reducing the burden on thermal baseload plants.25

## **The Hydro-Climate Crisis and the Strategic Pivot to Pumped Storage**

Hydropower, once the backbone of the Moroccan grid, has become a casualty of the country’s intensifying climate crisis. Chronic drought has reduced dam levels to historic lows, with some key reservoirs dropping to near 1% capacity at the beginning of 2024\.27 Consequently, the share of hydropower in the electricity generation mix plummeted to less than 2% in recent years, despite representing over 17% of installed capacity.27

### **Pumped Hydropower Storage (PHS) as Grid Anchor**

In response to the decline in conventional hydroelectric output and the increasing intermittency of solar and wind, the Office National de l'Électricité et de l'Eau Potable (ONEE) has redirected its hydro strategy toward Pumped Hydropower Storage (PHS).27 Unlike conventional dams that rely on seasonal rainfall, PHS functions as a grid-scale "water battery" by cycling water between upper and lower reservoirs.28

ONEE has established a goal of 1,000 MW of PHS capacity by 2030\.27 Key operational and pipeline projects include:

* **Abdelmoumen (350 MW):** Recently commissioned near Agadir, this plant provides essential peak-shaving and frequency regulation services.27  
* **Afourer (465 MW):** A long-standing facility that remains the largest operational reversible plant in the country.27  
* **Ifahsa (300 MW):** A high-priority project near Chefchaouen, supported by the World Bank, designed to facilitate the integration of 1.5 GW of new solar and wind by providing ancillary system services.28  
* **El Menzel (350 MW):** Currently in the development and financing stage, representing the next phase of the storage build-out.27

The Ifahsa PHS project is particularly illustrative of the system-level challenges. Located 14 km from Chefchaouen, it will feature two 150 MW reversible pump-turbine units.29 During off-peak hours, the plant will consume excess renewable energy from the grid to lift water, releasing it during evening peaks to ensure grid stability without relying on thermal spinning reserves.28

## **Institutional Reforms and the Evolution of Market Governance**

The Moroccan electricity sector is currently navigating its most significant institutional restructuring since the 1960s. Historically, the sector was characterized by a vertically integrated, state-owned model where ONEE acted as the single buyer and sole operator of the transmission and distribution networks.3 To improve financial sustainability and attract private capital, the government is implementing a multi-pillared reform program.

### **The Unbundling of ONEE and the Rise of SRMs**

A central component of this reform is the unbundling of ONEE’s activities. In 2024, the process of separating generation, transmission, and distribution began, with distribution being transferred to newly created Regional Multi-Service Companies (SRMs).3 The first SRM was established in October 2024, marking a shift toward a decentralized distribution model aimed at improving operational efficiency and facilitating local renewable energy integration.31

### **Legislative Frameworks: Law 13-09 to Law 82-21**

The legislative architecture has evolved to permit increasing private sector participation. Law 13-09, adopted in 2010, initially opened the high-voltage and very-high-voltage markets to private renewable energy producers, allowing them to sell directly to industrial end-consumers.3

However, the most transformative recent regulation is Law 82-21 on the self-production of electrical energy.3 Implemented through decrees in 2024, Law 82-21 formally allows individuals, legal entities, and industrial sites to generate renewable electricity for their own use and sell up to 20% of their annual surplus back to the grid.3 This "self-generation revolution" is expected to unlock the commercial and industrial (C\&I) solar market, which by mid-2025 had already added over 1 GW of capacity through rooftop and on-site installations.5

## **Grid Infrastructure and the Challenges of Variable Renewable Integration**

As variable renewables (VRE) like wind and solar approach 50% of installed capacity, the technical challenges of grid management have moved to the forefront of Moroccan energy policy. The geographical mismatch between generation centers and demand centers necessitates significant investment in transmission infrastructure.36

### **Transmission Bottlenecks and the HVDC North-South Link**

A 2022-2024 study of the Moroccan high-voltage network using the GridCal simulation framework identified that wind integration in the southern provinces has significantly increased power transfer requirements along the southern-north corridor.36 Specifically, wind expansion increased southern transfer flows from 55 MW to 83 MW, identifying critical bottlenecks that could lead to curtailment if not addressed.36

In response, ONEE is developing a 1,400 km North-South High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) "electricity highway" with a capacity of 3 GW.4 This project is designed to transmit massive volumes of renewable power from Dakhla and the southern provinces to the heavily populated northern industrial zones.4 In November 2024, ONEE shortlisted five international companies for the engineering, procurement, and construction (EPC) contract, signaling the project’s strategic priority.4

### **Regional Interconnections and Geopolitical Positioning**

Morocco’s grid strategy is inherently international. By leveraging its location at the crossroads of Africa and Europe, Morocco aims to become a regional green energy hub. It currently operates two subsea interconnectors with Spain, with a third 600 MW link scheduled for 2030\.4

The most ambitious proposed project was the Xlinks Morocco-UK Power Project, which sought to deliver 3.6 GW of renewable energy from Guelmim-Oued Noun to the UK via 4,000 km of subsea cables.4 Although the UK government withdrew policy backing in June 2025 due to risk concerns, the project continues as a private venture, highlighting the commercial allure of Morocco’s high solar intensity (34% solar load vs 11% in the UK) and consistent wind resources.25 Furthermore, the developer is reportedly exploring similar schemes between Morocco and Germany.25

## **The Green Hydrogen "Morocco Offer" and 2050 Long-Term Goals**

Looking toward the mid-century horizon, Morocco is positioning itself as a global leader in the industrial production and export of green hydrogen and its derivatives (ammonia and methanol).5 The "Morocco Offer," launched in March 2024, provides a structured framework for international developers, including land allocations and infrastructure guarantees.5

The green hydrogen roadmap is extremely ambitious, requiring the installation of 14.6 GW of additional renewable capacity by 2030 and up to 131.5 GW by 2050 to support domestic industry and exports.32 This strategy is designed to decarbonize Morocco’s massive phosphate and fertilizer industry, which is a significant consumer of imported ammonia, while simultaneously providing a new export revenue stream to balance the energy trade account.8

The 2050 Long-Term Low Emission Development Strategy (ST-LEDS) envisions a net-zero electricity sector by the 2050s, supported by the complete phase-out of coal, the mass deployment of storage (PHS and BESS), and the electrification of the transport and industrial sectors.1

## **Financial Sustainability and Sovereign Risk Management**

The transition’s success is inextricably linked to the financial health of the National Office of Electricity and Water (ONEE) and the kingdom’s ability to access international climate finance. ONEE has historically struggled with a lack of cost-reflective tariffs and entrenched cross-subsidies, leading to high debt levels.30

The International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank have provided critical support through facilities such as the Resilience and Sustainability Facility (RSF) and the Flexible Credit Line (FCL), totaling billions of dollars.31 These arrangements are conditioned on Morocco advancing structural reforms, including the liberalization of the electricity market, the gradual removal of butane gas subsidies, and the implementation of a medium-term fiscal framework.31 The successful first review of the RSF in March 2024 confirms Morocco’s commitment to these reforms, which are viewed as essential for maintaining macroeconomic stability during the capital-intensive phase of the transition.31

## **Conclusion: Synthesis and Strategic Outlook**

The Moroccan electricity system has entered a phase of mature diversification and regulatory modernization. The 2009 vision has successfully established a foundation where renewables provide nearly half of the country’s installed capacity, significantly reducing its reliance on imported oil.3 However, the transition from "installed capacity" to "generation dominance" remains a major challenge, complicated by the climate-driven decline of hydropower and the persistent need for coal-fired baseload power.27

The pivot toward solar PV-plus-storage (as seen in Midelt II and III) and the strategic emphasis on pumped storage represent an adaptive and pragmatic response to technological evolution and climate change.21 Furthermore, the decentralization of the market through Law 82-21 and the SRM restructuring will likely catalyze a new wave of private, smaller-scale investments that can reduce pressure on the national grid.33

Ultimately, Morocco’s trajectory is defined by a high-stakes alignment of climate policy, energy security, and industrial strategy. While infrastructure delays in the gas and interconnector sectors represent short-term headwinds, the kingdom's "double-bonus" of abundant solar and wind resources, combined with a robust institutional framework, positions it as a resilient and indispensable player in the future Mediterranean and African energy landscapes.4 The next decade will determine whether the "Morocco Offer" can successfully transform these natural resources into a sustainable and competitive green industrial economy.
