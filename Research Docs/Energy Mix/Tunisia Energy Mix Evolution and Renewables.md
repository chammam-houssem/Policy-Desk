# **The political economy of Tunisia’s energy transition: A system-level analysis of electricity mix evolution and renewable trajectories**

## **Executive summary**

Tunisia stands at a critical juncture in its energy history, shifting from a long-standing reliance on fossil fuels toward an ambitious but technically challenging renewable-led future. This transition is not merely a response to global climate imperatives but is driven primarily by an acute energy security crisis. Since the turn of the millennium, Tunisia has transitioned from a net energy exporter to a country heavily dependent on imports, particularly natural gas from Algeria, which now fuels approximately 95% of its electricity generation.1 This structural dependency has created significant fiscal vulnerabilities, as the state utility, Société Tunisienne de l'Électricité et du Gaz (STEG), faces mounting debt and the government struggles with unsustainable energy subsidies that have historically consumed nearly 9% of the national GDP.3

The evolution of Tunisia's electricity mix over the past two decades reveals a system that successfully achieved near-universal electrification but failed to diversify its primary energy sources in tandem with declining domestic hydrocarbon production.5 While the 1990s and early 2000s were defined by the consolidation of natural gas dominance, the post-2011 period has seen the slow but steady emergence of a renewable energy framework. The 2015 Renewable Energy Law and the subsequent Tunisian Solar Plan (PST) established the legal foundations for private sector participation, though administrative bottlenecks and grid constraints have historically caused a significant gap between policy targets and actual deployment.4

As of mid-2025, the Tunisian system is beginning to experience an acceleration. The commissioning of the 100 MW Metbasta solar plant in Kairouan and the approval of over 2.3 GW of new renewable tenders for 2026 signal a pivot toward utility-scale, privately financed projects.7 Furthermore, the strategic focus has expanded to include green hydrogen for export and high-voltage interconnections with Europe, such as the ELMED project.10 However, achieving the target of a 35% renewable share in the electricity mix by 2030 and 50% by 2035 will require overcoming deep-seated challenges in grid modernization, institutional reform, and the high cost of financing in a volatile macroeconomic environment.2 This report provides an exhaustive analysis of these dynamics, detailing the technological, policy, and systemic drivers shaping Tunisia's trajectory toward energy sovereignty.

## **Evolution of the energy mix from 2000 to the present**

The history of Tunisia's energy sector since 2000 is characterized by a widening gap between domestic production and surging demand, leading to a profound structural reliance on natural gas imports. In 1990, Tunisia's primary energy consumption was approximately 4,500 ktoe, a figure that nearly doubled to 8,500 ktoe by 2012, reflecting the country's rapid industrialization and improving living standards.3 However, domestic energy production failed to keep pace. While Tunisia was a net exporter of energy until 2000, it became a net importer thereafter, a shift that has defined its contemporary energy policy.3

### **The consolidation of natural gas dominance**

Since 2000, natural gas has effectively marginalized other thermal fuels in the electricity sector. In the late 20th century, oil and oil products played a more significant role in power generation, but their share has dwindled to negligible levels, reaching just 0.1% by 2023\.1 This shift was driven by the availability of domestic gas from fields such as El Borma and Hasdrubal, and more importantly, Tunisia's strategic role as a transit country for the Trans-Mediterranean (Transmed) pipeline, which delivers Algerian gas to Italy.5

The reliance on gas was further cemented by the construction of large-scale combined-cycle gas turbine (CCGT) plants, such as the ones in Sousse, Mornaguia, and Radès.16 These plants offered high efficiency and were seen as a modern solution to meeting an annual demand growth rate that fluctuated between 2% and 5%.4 By 2016, gas dependence for electricity generation had reached 97%, creating a "carbon lock-in" that made any deviation from this path both technically and financially difficult.18

### **The transition to import dependence**

The turning point for Tunisia's energy security arrived when domestic hydrocarbon production began a steady decline. Petroleum production peaked in the mid-1980s at 120,000 barrels per day (bbl/d) but fell to approximately 60,000 bbl/d by 2013\.6 Similarly, domestic gas production, which had been increasing until the early 2010s, has halved since its peak in 2010\.3 By 2014, only 49% of natural gas consumption was covered by domestic production, with the remainder imported from Algeria.3

This reliance has had severe fiscal implications. As the value of the Tunisian Dinar (TND) weakened against the US dollar and Euro, the cost of gas imports soared. Natural gas prices were eight times more expensive in 2012 than in 2000\.3 Because STEG has historically subsidized consumer electricity prices to maintain social stability, these rising import costs translated directly into a massive state deficit and high debt levels for the utility.3

### **Policy shifts and renewable emergence post-2010**

The 2011 Revolution served as a catalyst for reconsidering the national energy strategy. While the immediate post-revolutionary years were marked by political instability that delayed infrastructure projects, the period also saw a growing recognition that the status quo was unsustainable. The first major policy milestone was the 2015 Law on the Production of Electricity from Renewable Energy Sources.4 This law was designed to end STEG's monopoly on generation and create a framework for private investment through three primary regimes:

1. **Authorization Regime:** Targeting small and medium projects (typically \<10 MW for solar and \<30 MW for wind) for developers to sell power to STEG under a Power Purchase Agreement (PPA).5  
2. **Concession Regime:** Targeting large-scale utility projects (\>10 MW for solar, \>30 MW for wind) awarded through international competitive bidding.5  
3. **Self-Consumption Regime:** Allowing industrial, commercial, and residential actors to produce their own power and sell up to 30% of the surplus to STEG.4

Despite these legislative efforts, the 2015-2020 period saw relatively few projects reach the commissioning phase due to administrative resistance, grid integration challenges, and a lack of a clear regulatory body.4 It was only after 2020, as the fiscal crisis deepened and international pressure for decarbonization increased, that a new "acceleration phase" began, characterized by larger tenders and more robust international financing.7

## **Current energy mix snapshot: Latest data and structural mismatch**

As of early 2025, the Tunisian electricity sector remains dominated by natural gas, yet the capacity breakdown is starting to reflect the initial successes of the renewable energy program. It is critical to distinguish between *installed capacity share* and *electricity generation share*, as the mismatch between these two figures highlights both the intermittency of renewables and the historical delays in project commissioning.

As of June 2025, the total installed renewable energy capacity reached 787 MW, comprising 485 MW of solar, 240 MW of wind, and 62 MW of hydro.5 While this represents a significant increase from the roughly 400 MW installed in 2020, the generation share of renewables remains stubbornly low, at approximately 6%.5 This mismatch is partly due to the recent nature of many installations; for instance, the 100 MW Kairouan solar plant and several 50 MW plants only entered commercial operation or trials in late 2025, meaning their full contribution is not yet reflected in annual generation data.7

The total production capacity of the national system is 5,944 MW distributed across 25 power plants.5 STEG maintains a dominant role, controlling 92.1% of this capacity and accounting for 95% of generation, while private participants (primarily the Carthage Power Company and a growing number of small RE developers) produce the remainder.5 This centralized structure is under increasing pressure as the government seeks to mobilize $2.8 billion in private investment to add 2.8 GW of new capacity by 2028\.24

## **Solar development: Technological shifts and project trajectories**

Solar energy is the centerpiece of Tunisia's National Renewable Energy Plan. The country possesses an exceptional solar resource, with horizontal irradiation levels ranging from 1,800 to 2,600 kWh/m²/year, particularly in the southern governorates.8

### **The pivot from CSP to PV**

In the early years of the Tunisian Solar Plan (PST), there was significant debate over the role of Concentrated Solar Power (CSP) versus Photovoltaics (PV). Early strategy documents envisioned CSP as a critical tool for grid stability due to its thermal storage capabilities, which allow for generation during evening peak hours.27 However, the global collapse in the Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE) for PV has fundamentally changed the landscape. In the MENA region, solar PV LCOEs have dropped to approximately 1.09 USD cents/kWh, making it the most cost-competitive option for large-scale deployment.28

As a result, Tunisia's recent capacity additions and the 1.3 GW push scheduled for 2025-2026 are almost exclusively focused on utility-scale PV.8 To address the intermittency issues once solved by CSP, the government is now pairing PV projects with Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) and pumped hydro storage.7

### **Major solar projects and milestones**

The year 2025 marked a breakthrough for utility-scale solar in Tunisia. Several large-scale projects, developed under the concession regime, reached critical milestones:

* **Metbasta (Kairouan):** A 100 MWp (alternatively 120 MWp) plant developed by AMEA Power. It is the country's first large-scale privately financed solar project and entered commercial operation in December 2025\.7  
* **Sidi Bouzid and Tozeur:** Two 50 MW plants that finalized financing in late 2024 and were approximately 80% complete by the end of 2025\.7  
* **Mazouna:** A 50 MW plant that began trial operations in late 2025\.7  
* **Wadi Project (Gabès):** A 132 MW project awarded to Voltalia in early 2026, scheduled for commissioning in 2028\.19  
* **Bazma (Kebili):** A 350 MW solar PV plant with integrated battery storage, approved for tender for 2026\.7

### **The self-consumption and authorization regimes**

While large concession projects provide the bulk of the capacity, smaller-scale solar has been the most consistent driver of deployment to date. The self-consumption regime has proven popular for industrial actors and households, with nearly 400 MW of low-voltage PV capacity installed by early 2025\.29 This was significantly boosted by the 2021 decision to eliminate pre-authorization requirements for private projects under 1 MW.5

The authorization system (for 1–10 MW projects) has also seen activity, with 54 preliminary agreements totaling 261 MW issued as of 2025, and 15 projects already operational.29 This decentralized growth is crucial for reducing peak load pressure on STEG, although it presents new challenges for grid management at the distribution level.2

## **Wind development: Geographic distribution and expansion pipeline**

Wind energy currently provides approximately 240 MW of Tunisia's installed capacity.5 Historically, wind was the leading renewable technology in the country, largely because STEG pioneered development in the 2000s and early 2010s with sites like Bizerte and Nabeul.13

### **Geographic concentration and resource assessment**

Tunisia's wind resources are bifurcated. High-velocity winds are found in the northern coastal and mountainous regions (Bizerte, Nabeul, Zaghouan), while the southern regions (Kebili, Medenine, Gafsa) offer vast land areas with slightly lower but highly consistent wind profiles.7

### **Scaling up: The 2.3 GW renewable push**

In December 2025, the High Committee for Private Electricity Generation, chaired by the Prime Minister, approved an unprecedented expansion of the wind pipeline. Tenders for 2 GW of wind capacity were greenlit for 2026\.7 This surge in wind capacity is essential for meeting the 2030 target of 35% renewables, as wind generation typically offers a higher capacity factor than solar PV in Tunisia, providing more generation per MW of installed capacity.5

A landmark in this expansion was the 25-year Power Purchase Agreement signed in early 2026 by Scatec ASA and its partner Aeolus (Toyota Tsusho) for the 75 MW El Fahs wind facility.31 This project, estimated to cost €100 million, represents Scatec's first wind venture in Tunisia and signals growing confidence among international developers in the country’s tender model.31

## **Hydropower and storage: The critical role of grid stability**

Hydropower plays a minimal role in Tunisia's generation mix, contributing less than 1% of total electricity.5 The existing 62 MW of capacity is mostly concentrated in small-scale installations at dams in the north.5 However, the role of hydro is being redefined through the development of Pumped Hydro Energy Storage (PHES).

### **Oued El Melah pumped storage project (STEP)**

As Tunisia integrates higher shares of intermittent solar and wind, energy storage has become a priority. STEG is currently planning a 400–600 MW pumped hydro energy storage plant at Oued El Melah, near Tabarka.33 This facility will consist of two reservoirs at different elevations; during periods of surplus renewable generation, water is pumped to the upper reservoir, and during peak demand, it is released through turbines to generate electricity.23

The project, which has been under geological and geotechnical study since 2022 with support from Germany's KfW, is slated for a 2029 commissioning date.23 Once operational, the STEP is expected to save 250 ktoe of fuel annually and provide the grid with the flexibility needed to manage 4 GW of planned renewable capacity by 2030\.23

### **Battery storage and grid modernization**

The government has recognized that large-scale hydro projects like Oued El Melah have long development cycles. Consequently, it is aggressively promoting Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS). The 350 MW solar tender in Bazma (Kebili) specifically includes battery storage to support grid stability.7 The World Bank's TEREG program is also supporting STEG in pilot BESS deployment and the modernization of the grid code to allow for the effective integration of decentralized storage solutions.2

## **Policy framework and national targets: Strategic and climate goals**

Tunisia's energy transition is guided by a hierarchy of official documents, ranging from broad climate commitments to granular sector-specific strategies.

### **The Energy Strategy Synthesis for 2035**

Adopted in February 2023, this strategy provides the master plan for the sector. It aims for a renewable energy capacity of 8,350 MW by 2035, with the overarching goals of strengthening energy security, reducing fossil fuel imports, and achieving carbon neutrality by 2050\.35

### **Updated Nationally Determined Contributions (NDC)**

In 2021, Tunisia submitted a significantly more ambitious NDC to the UNFCCC. It committed to a 45% reduction in carbon intensity by 2030 compared to 2010 levels—a major increase from the 13% reduction pledged in its first NDC.2 Achieving this target requires an estimated $14.4 billion in investment, with the energy sector expected to provide 72% of the total greenhouse gas (GHG) reductions.19 Of the 45% reduction, 27% is unconditional (domestic effort), and 18% is conditional on international support.19

### **The National Green Hydrogen Strategy**

Launched in 2024, the green hydrogen strategy aims to leverage Tunisia's renewable resources for both industrial decarbonization and export. By 2050, Tunisia plans to produce 8.3 million tonnes of green hydrogen annually, with 6 million tonnes intended for export to Europe via the SoutH2 corridor.10 This strategy is expected to require an overall investment of €120 billion and create 430,000 jobs by 2050\.10

## **Future energy mix trajectory: Hub ambitions and interconnections**

Tunisia is planning to transition from a fossil-dependent importer to a regional green energy hub. This trajectory is defined by three major technological and infrastructure developments.

### **The ELMED Interconnection**

The ELMED project consists of a 200 km, 600 MW High-Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) submarine cable connecting Tunisia to Italy.11 Supported by the European Union, the World Bank, and various development banks, ELMED will integrate the North African and European grids.11 While proponents see it as a way to stabilize the Tunisian grid and export surplus renewable power, critics warn that it could increase Tunisia's debt and lead to a situation where domestic energy needs are sidelined in favor of export profits.14

### **Natural gas as a transition fuel**

In the medium term, natural gas will remain a critical part of the mix, but its role will shift from a primary baseload fuel to a flexible backstop for renewables.12 To support this, the government is proceeding with the development of the Skhira 1 and 2 CCGT plants (500 MW each), while also investigating the conversion of existing pipelines to carry hydrogen.10

### **Renewable scaling and technological composition**

By 2035, the electricity system is expected to be a balanced hybrid. Solar PV and wind will each provide approximately 20% of the total generation.5 The use of green hydrogen as a carrier for long-duration energy storage is also envisioned as a way to reduce gas consumption in the industrial and power sectors after 2030\.10

## **System-level drivers: Why the transition is accelerating**

Tunisia’s energy transition is driven by a unique confluence of economic, strategic, and political factors that have made the status quo untenable.

### **Energy security and sovereignty**

The primary driver is the existential threat of declining domestic hydrocarbons. With energy independence hovering around 40-48%, Tunisia is acutely vulnerable to international energy price volatility.14 The reliance on Algerian gas is no longer a strategic choice but a vulnerability that must be mitigated through the development of domestic renewable resources.2

### **Fiscal sustainability and utility performance**

The fiscal burden of energy subsidies—which hit $1.7 billion in 2013 and remained high in 2022-2023—is the main driver of the TEREG program.12 By accelerating renewable deployment, the World Bank expects to reduce electricity supply costs by 23%, improve STEG's cost recovery from 60% to 80%, and reduce state subsidies by TND 2.045 billion.24

### **Cost competitiveness of renewables**

The LCOE for solar and wind has dropped so dramatically that new renewable projects are now significantly cheaper than imported gas. Recent competitive bids for solar projects in Tunisia have ranged from 3.1 to 3.3 USc/kWh, a fraction of the cost of gas-fired generation once the full fiscal cost of imports and subsidies is considered.12

### **Industrial policy and green exports**

Tunisia views the green transition as an industrial opportunity. The green hydrogen strategy and the proximity to the European market via the Transmed and SoutH2 pipelines position Tunisia to become a major supplier of clean energy and ammonia to the EU.5 This is expected to attract billions in FDI and create a new high-tech industrial sector.5

## **Constraints and bottlenecks: Limiting factors and systemic risks**

Despite the robust drivers, several critical constraints threaten the speed and success of the transition.

### **Grid integration and infrastructure readiness**

The national grid requires massive investment to handle the variability of 4.8 GW of renewables by 2030\. STEG’s transmission and distribution (T\&D) networks currently suffer from 20% power losses.19 Projects like ESIP are building 384 km of new lines, but any delay in this infrastructure prevents the "evacuation" of power from new RE plants, a major cause of historical delays.12

### **Financing and the cost of equity**

The high risk profile of the Tunisian market remains a barrier. The cost of equity for renewables in Tunisia is 17%, compared to 7% in Germany.13 This high cost is driven by "power market risk" (regulation), "counterparty risk" (STEG’s reliability), and "political risk".13 Without the extensive use of concessional financing and international guarantees, many projects would remain unbankable.12

### **Institutional coordination and utility role**

There is a perceived conflict of interest in STEG’s dual role as a producer and the sole buyer of electricity from its private competitors.5 Critics argue that the utility has been slow to facilitate private investment to protect its own market share.4 The successful implementation of the 2035 strategy will depend on the establishment of a truly independent energy regulator, a goal that is still under development.12

### **Water scarcity and environmental stress**

Tunisia is a "water-stressed" nation, using 96% of its renewable water supply.41 This presents a major challenge for both CSP (which requires cooling) and green hydrogen (which requires high-purity water for electrolysis).41 The reliance on energy-intensive seawater desalination for these processes adds cost and complexity to the transition.41

## **Analytical synthesis: The political economy of change**

The evolution of Tunisia's electricity mix is a transition driven more by *energy security* and *fiscal necessity* than by environmental altruism. While the climate commitments (NDCs) provide the framework and international legitimacy for the transition, the immediate catalyst is the unsustainable cost of the gas-dependent status quo.

### **Why fossil generation remains dominant**

Fossil fuel generation remains dominant (95%) despite a 13% share in renewable capacity because of the "intermittency-capacity gap." Solar and wind plants operate with lower capacity factors than CCGT plants. Furthermore, until the Oued El Melah pumped storage project and utility-scale BESS are operational, STEG must maintain significant gas-fired spinning reserves to ensure grid frequency stability.23 This results in a system where renewable energy *capacity* must be built out far in excess of the *generation* share desired.

### **Explaining the target-deployment gap**

The persistent gap between Tunisia's ambitious targets and actual deployment can be explained by a "reform lag." The 2015 law created the framework, but the implementing decrees and PPA templates were not finalized until 2017\.4 Subsequent delays were caused by the lack of transmission infrastructure and the administrative complexity of the concession regime.8 The recent commissioning of the Kairouan plant suggests that these procedural hurdles are finally being overcome, provided that the state can maintain a predictable regulatory environment for foreign investors.8

### **Strategic Characterization**

Tunisia’s transition can be characterized as a "renewable expansion within a fossil-dominated, centralized system." The state utility, STEG, remains the pivot point of the entire transition, acting as both a technical guarantor of the grid and the primary fiscal offtaker.5 The move toward an export-driven model (ELMED, Hydrogen) represents a new strategy to leverage national resources for economic recovery, but it requires Tunisia to successfully navigate the transition from a closed, state-led energy economy to an open, regional energy player.

In summary, Tunisia’s energy trajectory is a race against its own fiscal and resource limits. The acceleration of 2025 provides evidence that the legislative and structural groundwork laid since 2015 is finally yielding utility-scale results. However, the path to 2030 and 2035 will depend on the successful execution of the TEREG and ELMED projects, the modernization of STEG, and the mobilization of billions in international capital to turn the country’s vast solar and wind potential into a new pillar of national economic sovereignty.
