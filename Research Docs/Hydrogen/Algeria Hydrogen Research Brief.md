# Algeria's hydrogen ambitions outpace its implementation capacity

Algeria has positioned hydrogen as the strategic bridge between its fossil-fuel-dependent present and a diversified energy future, but **the gap between announced ambition and on-the-ground reality remains vast**. As of April 2026, the country has zero operational green hydrogen capacity, no final investment decisions on any hydrogen project, and all activity remains at the MoU or feasibility-study stage. The national strategy, approved in December 2022, targets 40 TWh of hydrogen production by 2040 and envisions supplying 10% of Europe's hydrogen demand—objectives that would require a **$24.8 billion investment** and a transformation of Algeria's energy system that has not yet meaningfully begun.

This matters because Algeria possesses genuine structural advantages—the world's highest solar irradiance, existing gas pipeline infrastructure to Europe, proximity to demand centers, and a large ammonia industry already consuming grey hydrogen. Yet its renewable energy sector, after 12 years of targets, has installed only ~450 MW against an original 22 GW goal. The country's 50 MW pilot plant at Arzew, backed by €20 million in German funding, is the closest thing to a concrete project—and even this lacks a confirmed construction timeline. Algeria is best characterized as an **emerging but unproven hydrogen player**, trailing Morocco and Egypt in project execution while maintaining significant long-term potential contingent on renewable buildout and regulatory reform.

---

## 2. Algeria's strategic hydrogen positioning

Algeria frames hydrogen simultaneously as an **export diversification play**, a **geopolitical instrument**, and a tool for **industrial decarbonization**—though the export dimension dominates official discourse. Minister of Energy Mohamed Arkab has described Algeria as "a reliable energy partner to Europe for decades" that "possesses every asset needed to become a leading supplier of clean energy." This framing reveals the central logic: hydrogen extends Algeria's established role as a gas supplier into the post-fossil era, preserving the country's geopolitical leverage and revenue streams.

The institutional architecture reflects this strategic priority. The **Ministry of Energy and Mines** (now split into a Ministry of Hydrocarbons and Mines and a Ministry of Energy and Renewable Energies) developed and published the national strategy. **Sonatrach**, the state oil and gas company, is the lead industrial actor for virtually all hydrogen partnerships and pilot projects. **Sonelgaz**, the national utility, is a co-signatory of the ALTEH2A export consortium. **CEREFE** (Commission for Renewable Energies and Energy Efficiency), established in 2019 under the Prime Minister's office, provides advisory oversight. International technical assistance comes primarily from Germany's **GIZ** (through its TakatHy programme), **KfW** (financing the Arzew pilot), and **UNIDO/GEF** (through the Global Clean Hydrogen Programme).

Hydrogen is also part of Algeria's **rivalry with Morocco**. Morocco published its hydrogen roadmap in 2021, two years before Algeria, and has attracted more projects. Algeria's SoutH2 Corridor positions itself as a counterweight to Morocco's hydrogen ambitions, while the diplomatic rupture between the two countries (including the closure of the Maghreb-Europe gas pipeline in 2021) means they cannot cooperate on shared infrastructure. As Carnegie Endowment analysis notes, Algeria "uses energy to forge closer political and economic ties with Italy and Germany—and gain leverage with them."

## 3. Official plans, roadmaps, and targets

Algeria's **National Hydrogen Development Strategy** was approved by the Council of Ministers in December 2022 and publicly presented in March 2023 at a workshop organized by the Ministry of Energy and Mines. The strategy's full title is notable: *Feuille de route pour le développement de l'hydrogène renouvelable (vert) et propre (bleu) en Algérie*—covering both green and blue pathways. It was subsequently published in the Official Journal (Journal Officiel) in September 2023.

The strategy is organized in three phases with the following targets:

**Phase 1 (2023–2030): Pilot projects and framework-building.** Includes two Sonatrach pilot projects (2–4 MW electrolyzers with small-scale PV/wind), the 50 MW Arzew pilot plant (German-backed), and regulatory development. The goal is technological learning and institutional preparation.

**Phase 2 (2030–2040): Industrial scaling.** Targets **40 TWh total hydrogen production** (approximately 1 million tonnes), with **10 TWh for domestic use** and **30–40 TWh for export**. Aims for **10 GW of electrolysis capacity**, using a **70% solar PV / 30% wind** energy mix. The strategy envisions **$10 billion in annual export revenues** and supplying approximately **10% of European hydrogen demand** by 2040.

**Phase 3 (2040–2050): Full deployment.** Large-scale production and export of green or blue hydrogen in hard-to-electrify industrial sectors.

**These targets are strategic aspirations, not legally binding.** The strategy was adopted as a government-approved roadmap, not legislation with enforcement mechanisms. It explicitly states it "will be updated based on market developments and technological advances"—language of flexibility, not obligation. The most concrete legal instrument enacted so far is the **2026 Finance Law**, which introduced customs duty exemptions for electrolyzers and reduced duties on solar panel components. Minister Arkab stated in November 2025 that Algeria is "finalizing the legal and institutional framework for hydrogen activities," indicating that binding regulations are under development but not yet enacted.

The broader renewable energy context is critical: Algeria targets **15 GW of renewables by 2035** (revised downward from an original 22 GW by 2030 target), with a 27–30% share of electricity generation from renewables. A **$60 billion energy investment plan for 2025–2029** was announced in October 2025, encompassing oil, gas, renewables, and hydrogen.

## 4. Green hydrogen versus other pathways

The strategy title explicitly encompasses both green (renewable) and blue (clean/carbon-captured) hydrogen, but **green hydrogen overwhelmingly dominates actual activity**. All major international partnerships—ALTEH2A, Cepsa-Sonatrach, VNG cooperation, the German bilateral task force—focus exclusively on green hydrogen production via electrolysis powered by solar and wind. The SoutH2 Corridor is designated for renewable hydrogen transport.

Blue hydrogen (methane reforming with CCS) is mentioned as a Phase 1 option but has no announced projects, no CCS infrastructure under development, and no specific capacity targets. It functions more as a strategic placeholder—acknowledging Algeria's gas assets—than an active programme. No Algerian CCS project has been publicly identified, making blue hydrogen effectively theoretical.

**Derivatives and Power-to-X pathways are gaining attention** but remain nascent. The Cepsa-Sonatrach MoU specifically mentions **green ammonia and methanol** as target products. GIZ's PtX studies have evaluated the Arzew and Hassi R'Mel sites for Power-to-X applications. Academic analysis suggests Algeria's "optimal strategy is to export valorized hydrogen in its transformed forms, such as ammonia and e-fuels, owing to the significant added value and profitability." This makes economic sense: ammonia and methanol are easier to transport than gaseous hydrogen and have established global markets. However, **no quantified ammonia, methanol, or e-fuel production targets appear in the official strategy**.

Official documents do not provide precise technical definitions of "green hydrogen" or "blue hydrogen." The GIZ/RENAC programmes are working on **certification-of-origin frameworks** compatible with EU requirements, including the RFNBO (Renewable Fuels of Non-Biological Origin) criteria, but no mutual recognition agreement exists between the EU and Algeria.

## 5. Domestic demand and sectoral use cases

Algeria already has **substantial grey hydrogen consumption**—an underappreciated fact in most analyses. The country's ammonia and fertilizer industry is the primary consumer, with total ammonia production capacity of approximately **2.8–4+ million tonnes per year** across three major complexes. This implies **~0.5–0.7 million tonnes per year of grey hydrogen consumption** from steam methane reforming.

The three key facilities are **Sorfert** (OCI 51% / Sonatrach 49%) at Arzew, with 1.6 Mt/year ammonia and 1.26 Mt/year urea capacity, exporting 80% to Western Europe; **Fertial** (state-owned Asmidal), with combined capacity of ~1.2 Mt/year ammonia at Arzew and Annaba, undergoing revamping to reach 1.8 Mt/year; and **AOA** (Algerian-Omani Fertiliser Company), whose operational status remains unclear due to reported gas pricing disputes between Sonatrach and its Omani partner.

Beyond fertilizers, **refineries** consume hydrogen for hydrodesulfurization and hydrocracking across Sonatrach's facilities at Skikda, Arzew, Algiers, and Hassi Messaoud—though precise volumes are not publicly reported. **Steel** represents a newer potential demand source: Tosyali Algeria's DRI-based steel complex near Oran uses Midrex technology capable of operating with increasing hydrogen percentages, and a June 2025 quadripartite MoU between Sonatrach, Sonelgaz, Hecate Energy, and Tosyali targets green hydrogen for steel decarbonization. The **Ghar Djebilet iron ore project**, with a dedicated 200 MW solar plant, could eventually become a green steel hub.

The strategy targets **10 TWh of hydrogen for domestic use by 2040**, compared to 30–40 TWh for export. This 3:1 or 4:1 export-to-domestic ratio confirms that Algeria's hydrogen strategy is **primarily export-led**, with domestic industrial substitution as a secondary objective. However, the existing ammonia industry provides a genuinely bankable domestic demand floor—replacing grey hydrogen in fertilizer production is technically feasible, commercially logical, and would not require new end-use markets.

## 6. Project pipeline and implementation status

**Algeria has zero operational green hydrogen facilities, zero projects at or past final investment decision, and zero electrolyzer manufacturing capacity.** All hydrogen activity remains at the MoU or feasibility-study stage. The following inventory distinguishes between serious pipeline activity and preliminary announcements.

**Most concrete project: 50 MW Arzew pilot plant.** This German-Algerian project involves a 50 MW electrolyzer powered by solar PV at Arzew, Algeria's major LNG and petrochemical hub. KfW has committed **€20 million** in financing. A bilateral hydrogen task force was established in February 2024. However, no construction timeline has been confirmed, and limited progress has been publicly reported since the February 2024 announcement. This is the only project with committed public funding.

**Most strategically significant: ALTEH2A / SoutH2 Corridor.** Launched in October 2024 by Sonatrach, Sonelgaz, VNG (Germany), Snam and SeaCorridor (Italy), and VERBUND Green Hydrogen (Austria), this alliance aims to assess large-scale green hydrogen production in Algeria for export via the **3,300 km SoutH2 Corridor** pipeline to Italy, Austria, and Germany. The pipeline has **EU Project of Common Interest (PCI) status** and a five-country political declaration of support (January 2025). The feasibility study was formally launched in November 2025 in Berlin, with initial results expected by mid-2026. Pipeline capacity target is **4 million tonnes per year**. However, production sites in Algeria have not been identified, no engineering studies exist, and the corridor's operational target has already slipped from "2030" to "early 2030s."

**Other MoU-stage projects:**

- **Cepsa-Sonatrach** (October 2024): Feasibility study for integrated green hydrogen, methanol, and green ammonia production. No location, scale, or timeline defined.
- **Tosyali green steel** (June 2025): Quadripartite MoU between Sonatrach, Sonelgaz, Hecate Energy, and Tosyali for renewable hydrogen in steel production near Oran. Commercially logical given Tosyali's existing DRI capacity, but remains at feasibility stage.
- **Eni-Sonatrach BRN pilot** (MoU signed May 2022): Green hydrogen pilot at Bir Rebaa North using existing 10 MW solar PV. More than three years after the MoU, no progress toward construction or FID has been reported. Appears effectively stalled.
- **John Cockerill** (September 2024): Exploratory meetings only on electrolyzer technology. No formal MoU or project.

**Research-stage concepts:** MedHySol (Mediterranean Hydrogen Solar) has been an academic framework since 2005 without producing physical infrastructure. HySolThane (hydrogen-enriched natural gas for transport) remains a concept without operational evidence.

**Sonatrach's hydrogen portfolio** is extensive on paper—MoUs with VNG, Eni, Cepsa, Hecate, Tosyali, and participation in ALTEH2A—but the company has not progressed any project to FID. Sonatrach has allocated approximately **$1 billion** for energy transition projects including hydrogen pilots, though its primary business and capital allocation remain focused on oil and gas production.

## 7. Renewables, electricity system, and hydrogen linkages

Algeria's hydrogen ambitions are fundamentally constrained by its **renewable energy deficit**. The country generates **99% of its electricity from natural gas**, with only approximately **450–600 MW of renewable capacity installed** (mostly small-scale solar PV, plus 10 MW of wind at Adrar). In 2023, Algeria added just 11.17 MW of solar PV—a pace that renders GW-scale electrolysis impossible for years.

The mismatch between ambition and execution is stark. Algeria's original 2011 target of **22 GW of renewables by 2030** has been revised to **15 GW by 2035**, requiring ~1,000 MW of annual additions—a **100-fold acceleration** from the 11 MW added in 2023. The first serious acceleration has occurred: **3,200 MW of solar contracts** were awarded in 2023–2024 through Sonelgaz-EnR, including a 1,000 MW plant in M'Sila ($1.2 billion, targeting mid-2026 commissioning) and a 200 MW Masdar consortium project. Nine PV plants totalling 1,480 MW are targeted for commissioning by August 2026.

**No dedicated renewable projects for hydrogen exist yet.** All awarded solar capacity is for grid electricity. The 50 MW Arzew pilot would be the first renewable installation linked to hydrogen production. This creates a fundamental tension: Algeria's domestic electricity demand is growing **~6% annually**, with peak demand reaching 19.1 GW in 2024 and expected to reach 21.5 GW in 2025. Sonelgaz is investing **$4.94 billion in 2025** (a 56% increase) to upgrade grid infrastructure, including a GE Vernova order for 134 substations by 2028.

Even under optimistic renewable deployment scenarios, **most new renewable capacity will be absorbed by growing domestic electricity demand** and efforts to free up natural gas for export. Surplus renewable electricity available for electrolysis-based hydrogen production would remain minimal through at least 2030. The grid was built for centralized gas-fired generation; integrating GW-scale intermittent solar in desert regions far from demand centres requires major transmission upgrades that are only beginning.

Hydrogen is therefore best understood not as a near-term complement to renewable expansion but as a **long-term aspiration dependent on renewable capacity exceeding domestic electricity needs**—a threshold Algeria is unlikely to reach before the mid-2030s at the earliest.

## 8. Water constraints and environmental considerations

Algeria is a **water-scarce country** with renewable water resources of approximately 450 m³ per capita per year—below the World Bank's **500 m³ scarcity threshold**. The current water deficit is estimated at **1.3 billion cubic metres**. Northern aquifers are exploited at ~90% of capacity, and southern Saharan aquifers are fossil and non-renewable. Climate projections suggest rainfall could decrease 20% by 2050.

The water-hydrogen nexus is significant but poorly integrated into official planning. Electrolysis requires approximately **9–10 litres of ultrapure water per kilogram of hydrogen**. Algeria's 2040 target of 30–40 TWh of hydrogen production would require **tens of millions of cubic metres of water annually**, with seawater desalination as the only viable source for large-scale production.

Algeria has invested substantially in desalination, operating **17–25 large-scale seawater reverse osmosis plants** along the Mediterranean coast with combined capacity of approximately **2.2 million m³/day**—one of Africa's largest desalination networks. Desalination currently provides ~17% of national drinking water, with a target of 42%. Five additional plants are planned or under construction. However, desalination is energy-intensive (**3.5–5 kWh/m³**), creating a circular challenge: producing hydrogen requires water, which requires desalination, which requires electricity, which must itself come from renewables if the hydrogen is to be classified as green.

**No integrated water-hydrogen planning framework exists.** This is a significant policy gap. Official strategy documents acknowledge water constraints but do not specify water sources, allocations, or the trade-offs between hydrogen production, domestic water supply, and agriculture. Multiple academic studies emphasize that "in Algeria, green hydrogen cannot be discussed without desalination." Regional differences are also important: optimal solar resources are in the southern Sahara, where water is most scarce, while desalination infrastructure is concentrated along the northern Mediterranean coast. This geographic mismatch adds complexity to siting decisions for hydrogen production.

## 9. Export strategy, infrastructure, and geopolitics

Algeria's hydrogen export strategy centres on the **SoutH2 Corridor**, a 3,300 km pipeline running from Algeria through Tunisia and Sicily to mainland Italy, Austria, and Germany. The corridor—with target capacity of **4 million tonnes of hydrogen per year** (approximately 40% of the EU's REPowerEU import target)—has received EU Project of Common Interest status and a five-country political declaration of support. Approximately 65–70% of the corridor would use repurposed existing gas pipelines, with new segments where necessary. The Italian section alone is estimated at ~€4 billion, with total corridor costs exceeding €10 billion.

The existing pipeline infrastructure is a genuine competitive advantage. The **Trans-Mediterranean pipeline** (Enrico Mattei/TransMed) runs from Hassi R'Mel through Tunisia to Sicily and onward to northern Italy, carrying ~32 bcm/year of gas. Snam and SeaCorridor (an Eni-Snam joint venture) explicitly reference hydrogen transport potential. However, the pipeline currently carries critical gas flows—Algeria supplies ~36% of Italy's gas imports—meaning conversion would need to be carefully phased.

**Spain has effectively excluded Algeria from its hydrogen corridor plans.** Despite Medgaz carrying 10.5 bcm/year of gas from Algeria to Spain, Madrid dropped plans to convert the pipeline for hydrogen, pivoting instead to the H2Med pipeline (Barcelona–Marseille). This is a notable geopolitical development, partly driven by strained Algeria-Spain relations following Spain's 2022 recognition of Morocco's Western Sahara autonomy plan. The Cepsa-Sonatrach MoU targets the "European market" generally, not specifically the Spanish corridor.

**Italy is Algeria's primary hydrogen partner**, a relationship strengthened by Italy's post-Ukraine energy diversification and the Meloni government's Mattei Plan for Africa. Algeria became Italy's largest gas supplier in 2022–2023, with trade reaching $15.9 billion in 2024. Over 40 bilateral agreements were signed at the July 2025 Italy-Algeria Intergovernmental Summit. **Germany** is the primary technical and financial partner, providing KfW financing for the Arzew pilot and hosting the ALTEH2A feasibility process. **Austria** participates through VERBUND Green Hydrogen and corridor transit infrastructure. **France** has minimal hydrogen engagement with Algeria.

**Comparative positioning against North African competitors is unfavourable on execution.** Morocco published its hydrogen roadmap in 2021, has 17 hydrogen projects in the IEA database (compared to Algeria's 2), and benefits from a more favourable business environment and EU political alignment. Egypt leads with 39 projects, including Fertiglobe's operational green ammonia plant and a successful H2Global auction award. Algeria is notably absent from the **African Green Hydrogen Alliance**, whose members include Morocco, Egypt, Mauritania, Namibia, South Africa, and Kenya. Estimated levelized costs of hydrogen are comparable across North Africa (Algeria: $1.72–3.23/kg by 2050 vs. Morocco: $1.73–3.28/kg), but project execution capacity is the differentiator.

**Port infrastructure** at Arzew/Bethioua—Algeria's major LNG and petrochemical complex handling ~3,000 vessels and 67 million tonnes annually, with existing ammonia production—is the most logical hub for future hydrogen or ammonia exports. Skikda's refinery and LNG infrastructure provides a secondary option.

## 10. Political economy constraints and implementation risks

**Fossil fuel subsidies are the single largest structural barrier** to hydrogen development. Algeria's household electricity price is approximately **$0.038/kWh**—one-seventh of UK prices—and more than **60% below cost-recovery levels** (IMF, 2023). Natural gas prices are more than 90% below cost recovery. Total energy subsidies reach approximately **$17 billion annually, or ~6% of GDP**. These subsidies make green hydrogen fundamentally uncompetitive in the domestic market and eliminate any price signal that would incentivize industrial users to switch from grey to green hydrogen. Subsidy reform is politically explosive in Algeria, where social stability depends partly on low-cost energy.

**The renewable energy implementation gap is the most telling constraint.** Algeria took 12 years (2011–2023) to install ~450 MW of renewables against a 22 GW target—achieving roughly 2% of its goal. The awarding of 3.2 GW of solar contracts in 2023–2024 represents the first genuine acceleration, but commissioning these projects will test an execution capacity that has historically underperformed. Until renewables are deployed at scale, green hydrogen production is physically impossible.

**The investment climate creates additional friction.** The US State Department's 2025 assessment describes a "difficult business climate, inconsistent regulatory environment, and sometimes contradictory government policies." The **51/49 rule** was eliminated for most sectors in 2020 but **retained for strategic sectors including energy and mining**. BP and Equinor have exited Algeria, and recent hydrocarbon licensing rounds attracted "underwhelming participation." State-owned banks control over 80% of banking sector assets, limiting private sector financing. Corruption remains a major challenge. These factors collectively deter the international private capital needed for a $24.8 billion hydrogen programme.

**Institutional coordination weaknesses** compound the challenge. The 2022 ministerial restructuring created dual ministries with overlapping hydrogen mandates. The AOA fertilizer plant—completed around 2015—remained non-operational for years due to gas pricing disputes between Sonatrach and its Omani partner, illustrating how state coordination failures can stall even completed infrastructure. Sonatrach itself, while operationally capable, is primarily focused on maintaining oil and gas production, with hydrogen as a secondary and longer-term priority.

The key constraints, ranked by severity:

- **Energy subsidies**: Eliminate domestic competitiveness of green hydrogen
- **Renewable buildout lag**: Physical impossibility of green hydrogen without renewables at scale
- **Regulatory immaturity**: No hydrogen-specific legislation, no PPA framework, no certification scheme
- **Investment climate**: FDI barriers, regulatory unpredictability, strategic-sector restrictions
- **Water stress**: Structural deficit requiring energy-intensive desalination for hydrogen production

## 11. Credibility assessment and outlook

Algeria's hydrogen sector sits firmly in the **"emerging but aspirational"** category—somewhere between narrative-building and genuine institution-building, but far from bankable implementation. The credibility picture breaks down as follows:

**What is real today:** A government-approved strategy document with phased targets. A growing web of MoUs with credible European partners (VNG, Snam, VERBUND, Cepsa, Eni). Committed public funding (€20 million) for a single 50 MW pilot plant. EU PCI status for the SoutH2 Corridor and five-country political backing. A large existing ammonia industry that provides genuine domestic grey hydrogen demand (~0.5–0.7 Mt/year) available for substitution. Nascent customs and fiscal incentives through the 2026 Finance Law. 3.2 GW of awarded solar contracts representing the first real renewable acceleration.

**What remains aspirational:** The 40 TWh / 1 Mt production target by 2040. The $10 billion annual export revenue goal. The 10 GW electrolysis capacity target. The 10% share of European hydrogen demand. These figures exist in strategy documents but are not backed by bankable projects, financing commitments at scale, or a demonstrated ability to deploy renewables at the required pace.

Algeria is **not among the most serious hydrogen players in North Africa** when measured by project execution. A 2025 ScienceDirect analysis found that "no hydrogen projects have been publicly reported to have progressed to the feasibility study stage" in Algeria, compared to multiple projects in Morocco and Egypt that have advanced to engineering, procurement, and even commissioning. Egypt won Germany's H2Global auction for ammonia exports; Algeria has not participated. Algeria is absent from the African Green Hydrogen Alliance.

**The most credible near-term pathways (5–10 years)** are: (1) commissioning the 50 MW Arzew pilot as a technology demonstration, likely by 2028–2029 if current momentum holds; (2) completing the ALTEH2A feasibility study and potentially progressing to a first industrial-scale production project in the Arzew/Oran corridor by the early 2030s; (3) partial greening of existing ammonia production, leveraging newly commissioned solar capacity; and (4) the SoutH2 Corridor's Italian and Austrian pipeline segments becoming operational, creating a transport pathway that Algeria can eventually feed into.

**The determining factor is renewable energy deployment.** If Algeria's 3.2 GW solar programme delivers on schedule and subsequent tranches follow, hydrogen production becomes physically possible by the late 2020s. If renewable deployment again stalls—as it has for the past 12 years—Algeria's hydrogen strategy will remain on paper regardless of how many MoUs are signed.

| **Lead institutions** | Ministry of Energy and Mines (split into two ministries); Sonatrach (lead industrial actor); Sonelgaz (electricity/grid); CEREFE (advisory); GIZ and KfW (international technical/financial support) |
| **Major domestic demand sectors** | Ammonia/fertilizers (~0.5–0.7 Mt/yr grey H₂ substitution potential); refining; steel (Tosyali DRI complex); power balancing (aspirational) |
| **Main export orientation** | Pipeline hydrogen to Europe via SoutH2 Corridor (Italy → Austria → Germany); green ammonia and methanol as derivatives; Italy and Germany as primary partners; Spain effectively excluded |
| **Key projects** | 50 MW Arzew pilot (€20M KfW funding, pre-construction); ALTEH2A/SoutH2 Corridor (feasibility study launched Nov 2025); Cepsa-Sonatrach derivatives (MoU); Tosyali green steel (MoU); Eni BRN pilot (stalled) |
| **Main constraints** | Fossil fuel subsidies eliminating domestic competitiveness; 12-year renewable deployment lag; no hydrogen legislation; challenging FDI environment (51/49 rule retained); water scarcity; grid infrastructure gaps; institutional fragmentation |
| **Overall credibility rating** | **Emerging but aspirational.** Genuine structural advantages (solar, pipelines, proximity, existing ammonia demand) offset by severe implementation deficits. Behind Morocco and Egypt on project execution. Strategy is real; bankable implementation is not yet evident. The renewable buildout trajectory over 2025–2028 will determine whether hydrogen becomes feasible or remains on paper |
