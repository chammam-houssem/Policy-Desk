# **National Strategy and Market Evolution: A Comprehensive Research Brief on Egypt’s Hydrogen Economy**

## **Executive Summary**

The Arab Republic of Egypt is currently undergoing a structural pivot in its national energy architecture, transitioning from a traditional hydrocarbon-centric model toward a diversified, low-carbon energy hub. This strategic shift is anchored by the National Low-Carbon Hydrogen Strategy, finalized and launched in mid-2024, which seeks to capitalize on the country’s unique convergence of geographic, infrastructural, and natural resource advantages.1 The primary objective is to capture between 5% and 8% of the global tradable hydrogen market by 2040, a target projected to contribute approximately $18 billion to the national Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and catalyze the creation of over 100,000 local jobs.1

The Egyptian hydrogen narrative is fundamentally export-driven, primarily oriented toward the European Union’s decarbonization requirements as outlined in the REPowerEU framework.5 However, the strategy also incorporates significant industrial upgrading goals, particularly within the fertilizer and steel sectors, which currently rely on carbon-intensive "grey" hydrogen.6 The government has established a robust institutional framework, led by the National Council for Green Hydrogen, and supported by landmark legislation such as Law No. 2 of 2024, which provides some of the most competitive investment incentives in the Middle East and North Africa (MENA) region.8

Despite this high level of political ambition, the sector faces a "conversion gap" between preliminary agreements and finalized projects. While the portfolio of Memoranda of Understanding (MoUs) exceeds $130 billion, actual implementation is concentrated in a few frontrunner projects like the "Egypt Green" initiative in the Suez Canal Economic Zone (SCZONE).10 Critical constraints include exceptionally high domestic borrowing rates (16–18%), a significant mismatch between the 180 GW of potential renewable land allocation and the current 36 GW grid transmission capacity, and the technical necessity of large-scale seawater desalination in a water-stressed environment.12 This report analyzes the dual-track reality of Egypt's hydrogen sector: a mature policy and regulatory environment contrasted with a nascent and high-risk project implementation landscape.

## **Egypt’s Strategic Hydrogen Positioning**

Egypt’s positioning of hydrogen is not merely an environmental policy but a core component of its geopolitical and macroeconomic strategy. The state frames hydrogen as the third pillar of its energy history, following its roles as a major oil producer and a regional natural gas hub.14

### **The Geopolitical and Export Strategy**

The foremost strategic frame for Egyptian hydrogen is its role as an export powerhouse for the European market. This is driven by Egypt’s proximity to the Mediterranean and its existing energy diplomacy through the Eastern Mediterranean Gas Forum (EMGF).13 By positioning itself as a reliable supplier of green molecules—specifically green ammonia and e-methanol—Egypt seeks to replace its declining natural gas export surplus with higher-value renewable derivatives.5 The strategy is aligned with European geopolitical realignments following the 2022 energy crisis, aiming to secure long-term foreign direct investment (FDI) and stabilize the national balance of payments.5

In the maritime domain, the Suez Canal serves as the primary strategic asset. The government intends to transform the canal into a "Green Shipping Corridor," providing bunkering services for the 12% of global maritime trade that traverses the waterway.18 This elevates Egypt from a passive transit host to a value-added service provider, leveraging its control over the canal to anchor domestic production hubs.12

### **Industrial Upgrading and Energy Security**

While export is the primary driver, hydrogen is increasingly framed as an industrial upgrading tool. Egypt possesses a world-scale fertilizer industry that is currently the world’s sixth-largest producer of urea.6 Transitioning this sector to green hydrogen serves a dual purpose: it ensures the long-term competitiveness of Egyptian exports in a world of carbon border taxes (like the EU's CBAM) and reduces the domestic industrial sector’s reliance on the nation’s finite natural gas reserves.5

Furthermore, hydrogen is positioned as an energy security instrument. The recent decline in production from major gas fields, such as Zohr, has shifted Egypt toward becoming a net gas importer during peak periods.5 Integrating green hydrogen allows the state to buffer the domestic electricity and industrial sectors against fossil fuel price volatility and production shortfalls.5

### **Leading Public Institutions**

The governance of the hydrogen sector is characterized by a centralized "single-window" approach designed to reduce bureaucratic friction for international investors.

| Institution | Primary Function in Hydrogen Governance |

## **Official Plans, Roadmaps, and National Targets**

The formalization of Egypt’s hydrogen ambitions reached a milestone in August 2024 with the launch of the National Low-Carbon Hydrogen Strategy.1 This strategy replaces earlier fragmented announcements with a structured pathway through 2040\.

### **Production and Capacity Targets**

The strategy utilizes two primary developmental scenarios: the "Central" and "Green" scenarios. These targets represent strategic intentions and are not currently legally binding, though they serve as the basis for all official planning and infrastructure investment.4

| Target Metric (2040 Horizon) | Central Scenario | Green Scenario |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Annual Hydrogen Production** | 3.8 million tons 1 | 5.6 million tons 1 |
| **Global Trade Market Share** | 5% 4 | 8% 4 |
| **Installed Electrolyser Capacity** | 48 GW 4 | 76 GW 4 |
| **Required Renewable Capacity** | 72 GW 4 | 114 GW 4 |
| **Hydrogen-Related FDI (Estimated)** | \~$24 billion 4 | \~$34 billion 4 |
| **Annual GDP Contribution** | \~$10 billion 14 | \~$18 billion 1 |

For the medium-term (2030), the strategy sets a production target of 1.5 million tons per year under the central scenario, with 1.4 million tons designated specifically for export.4 This implies that in the initial decade, over 93% of the sector's output is intended to leave the country, highlighting the export-centric nature of the current policy.12

### **The Three-Phase Implementation Roadmap**

Egypt has adopted a phased approach to manage the transition from a nascent sector to a mature market participant 4:

1. **Experimental / Pilot Phase (2022–2030):** Characterized by state support for initial large-scale pilot projects, the establishment of the legislative framework (Law No. 2 of 2024), and the signing of framework agreements with "first-mover" developers.4  
2. **Scale-up Phase (2030–2040):** Focuses on achieving economies of scale, lowering the Levelized Cost of Hydrogen (LCOH), and developing domestic manufacturing capabilities for electrolysers and renewable components.4  
3. **Full Market Implementation (2040 and beyond):** Maintains Egypt’s global market position while pivoting toward deep decarbonization of the domestic power grid and hard-to-abate industries.4

### **Investment Framework and Law No. 2 of 2024**

To transform strategic intent into bankable projects, Egypt enacted Law No. 2 of 2024, which provides comprehensive incentives for projects where at least 70% of the investment is funded by foreign currency from outside Egypt.9

* **Tax Rebates:** Projects are eligible for a cash incentive (tax credit) ranging from 33% to 55% of the tax paid on income generated from the project.5  
* **VAT and Customs Exemptions:** Equipment, raw materials, and transport vehicles are exempt from Value Added Tax (VAT), and exports of hydrogen and derivatives are subject to a 0% VAT rate.5  
* **Land and Port Fees:** A 25% reduction on land usufruct fees and a 20% reduction on port-related fees and storage charges are granted for qualified projects.5  
* **The Golden License:** Qualified projects receive a single, comprehensive approval covering all stages from construction to operation, bypassing traditional multi-layered licensing.9

## **Green Hydrogen versus Other Pathways**

The Egyptian strategy is categorized under the broader umbrella of "Low-Carbon Hydrogen," which allows for a technology-neutral approach while clearly prioritizing green pathways for the European export market.1

### **Definitions and Terminology**

Official documents and strategies distinguish between several key terms, reflecting the country’s intent to play across the full spectrum of "Power-to-X" (PtX) pathways 5:

* **Green Hydrogen:** Defined as hydrogen produced through the electrolysis of water using 100% renewable energy (solar and wind).8  
* **Low-Carbon Hydrogen:** An inclusive term that covers both green hydrogen and blue hydrogen (natural gas with CCS), provided the carbon intensity meets specific standards.1  
* **Green Ammonia:** Ammonia synthesized using green hydrogen and nitrogen, serving as the primary carrier for long-distance transport and fertilizer production.4  
* **Synthetic Fuels (e-Fuels):** Includes green methanol and other derivatives targeted at the maritime and aviation sectors.9

### **The Role of Blue Hydrogen**

Unlike some North African peers who focus exclusively on green hydrogen, Egypt views blue hydrogen as a meaningful medium-term pathway. This is due to its existing natural gas infrastructure and favorable geology for carbon sequestration.13

Research into the Nile Delta's offshore and onshore sedimentary basins has identified significant potential for ![][image1] storage in depleted sandstone reservoirs.13 A comprehensive screening study identified 16 potential storage sites with a maximum estimated injection capacity of 622 million metric tons of ![][image1].32 This allows Egypt to theoretically produce blue hydrogen at a cost-competitive rate compared to green hydrogen in the near term, provided it can navigate the European Union’s evolving standards on low-carbon fuel imports.15

### **Ammonia as the Lead Carrier**

The strategy is overwhelmingly focused on ammonia as the primary transport medium. This is a pragmatic choice based on the ease of liquification and the existing global trade infrastructure for ammonia.4 Egypt’s strategy aims to integrate green hydrogen into its existing ammonia facilities, such as the EBIC plant in Ain Sokhna, creating a hybrid "brownfield" transition where green ammonia is blended with conventional production.6

## **Domestic Demand and Sectoral Use Cases**

The Egyptian hydrogen strategy, while currently export-led, identifies several critical domestic sectors that will drive demand in the medium to long term.

### **Fertilizers and Chemicals**

The most immediate and substantial domestic demand source is the fertilizer industry. Egypt’s reliance on urea exports makes it vulnerable to the EU’s Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM).5

* **Existing Consumption:** Egypt currently consumes significant volumes of "grey" hydrogen for ammonia production.6  
* **Substitution:** The "Egypt Green" project demonstrates the model of substitution, where 100 MW of electrolysis provides feedstock to replace natural gas-derived hydrogen in the production of 74,000 tons of ammonia annually.33

### **Steel and Heavy Industry**

Egypt is the Middle East's leading producer of crude steel, an industry that accounts for a large portion of national industrial emissions.7

* **Green Steel Initiatives:** Major players like Ezz Steel are already implementing international environmental standards (ISO 14001, 50001\) and monitoring ![][image1] intensity.35  
* **H-DRI Potential:** The transition to Hydrogen Direct Reduction of Iron (H-DRI) is a key policy goal. Kandil Steel has partnered with the International Finance Corporation (IFC) to develop a decarbonization program, supported by a $25 million loan to improve environmental practices and prepare for future fuel-switching.7

### **Shipping and Maritime Bunkering**

The Suez Canal creates a "captive" demand market. The General Authority for the SCZONE is localizing green fuel industries specifically to supply ships.19

* **Green Methanol:** The first successful bunkering of a container ship (Maersk) with green methanol in East Port Said in 2023 serves as the commercial benchmark for this sector.19  
* **Green Ammonia Bunkering:** Framework agreements with consortia like EDF Renewables and AMEA Power target the production of ammonia specifically for ship supply purposes in the Ain Sokhna area.11

### **Power and Transport**

The role of hydrogen in the domestic power and transport sectors is currently secondary. While hydrogen can assist in power grid balancing, Egypt is currently prioritizing battery storage (1.5 GW of planned storage) and pumped hydropower.23 In transport, the focus is on heavy-duty and maritime applications rather than light passenger vehicles.8

### **The Frontrunner: Egypt Green**

The "Egypt Green" project is the most credible implementation in the region. It has secured a €35 million grant from the EU and was the winner of the first H2Global pilot auction.33 This project is significant because it has a 20-year offtake agreement for shipping green ammonia to the Port of Rotterdam between 2027 and 2033, which provides the bankability that many other planned projects currently lack.20

### **Pipeline Challenges**

While the government has signed over 30 MoUs and 10 binding framework agreements, only about 13% of hydrogen projects globally are backed by binding offtake contracts.12 In Egypt, most projects remain in the feasibility or pre-FEED stage, with developers waiting for greater clarity on global demand and local infrastructure costs before committing to FIDs.12

## **Renewables, Electricity System, and Hydrogen Linkages**

Egypt’s hydrogen strategy is fundamentally a "Renewables Pull" strategy, where the abundant solar and wind resources are the primary driver of investment.41

### **Renewable Capacity and Land Allocation**

The state has allocated approximately 42,000 square kilometers (an area larger than Belgium) to the New and Renewable Energy Authority (NREA) for wind and solar projects.24

* **Resource Potential:** The Gulf of Suez offers world-class wind speeds (avg 10.5 m/s), while Upper Egypt has exceptionally high solar irradiance (over 3,500 hours of sunshine per year).16  
* **The Target-Capacity Gap:** To meet the 2030 hydrogen targets, Egypt needs roughly 19 GW to 41 GW of new renewable capacity dedicated solely to hydrogen production.4 This represents a nearly fivefold increase from the 7.7 GW currently installed.16

### **Grid Integration and "Energy Enclaves"**

A major tension exists between using renewables to decarbonize the national grid and using them for hydrogen exports.

* **The "Energy Enclave" Risk:** To meet EU "additionality" requirements, many developers are proposing dedicated, isolated renewable sites directly connected to electrolysers in Free Economic Zones.5 This risks creating "energy enclaves" where the best land and resources are used for export while the domestic grid remains fossil-fuel dependent.5  
* **Grid Modernization:** The EU-funded "Egypt Grid Modernisation and Expansion" programme (€90 million) is specifically designed to upgrade the transmission network to handle 22 GW of new clean energy by 2030\.38 This is a critical prerequisite, as the current grid can reportedly only handle 36 GW of capacity despite the 180 GW of potential land allocation.12

## **Water Constraints and Environmental Considerations**

Hydrogen production is water-intensive, requiring approximately 9 to 11 tons of high-purity water for every ton of hydrogen produced. In Egypt, this presents a severe technical and environmental challenge.20

### **The Desalination Mandate**

Egypt’s official strategy treats water availability as a major constraint and has mandated that no freshwater from the Nile or finite fossil aquifers can be used for electrolysis.13

* **Seawater Desalination:** All major projects in the SCZONE are planned to utilize seawater desalination. The SCZONE is implementing a "shared infrastructure" model where desalination plants serve multiple hydrogen facilities to lower the unit cost.17  
* **Energy-Water Trade-off:** To maintain the "green" certification of the final hydrogen product, the desalination process itself must be powered by renewable energy.13 This adds significantly to the CAPEX and electricity requirements of hydrogen projects.13

### **Environmental and Resource Management**

The Nile Delta aquifer is already undergoing significant storage loss (0.32 cm/year), and regional groundwater depletion is a major concern.42 Official policies emphasize the need for "win-win" investments that include water security for local communities alongside industrial use.41 However, the management of brine—the concentrated salt byproduct of desalination—remains a technical gap that developers must address to meet international environmental standards.17

## **Export Strategy, Infrastructure, and Geopolitics**

Egypt’s hydrogen strategy is the centerpiece of its "Energy Diplomacy," aimed at cementing its role as the primary link between European energy demand and African/Middle Eastern supply.5

### **Connection to Europe**

Egypt is leveraging several proposed and existing infrastructure corridors to reach European markets.

* **Maritime Corridors:** Ammonia shipping from Ain Sokhna to ports like Rotterdam and Venice is the most advanced pathway.23  
* **Pipeline Ambitions:** There are long-term plans for a "Mediterranean Hydrogen Bridge," which would involve either repurposing existing natural gas pipelines or constructing new hydrogen-ready sub-sea interconnections to Greece or Italy.13  
* **The T-MED Initiative:** The Trans-Mediterranean Renewable Energy and Clean-Tech Cooperation (T-MED) initiative, launched by the EU in 2026, aims to coordinate billions in private investment and regulatory convergence across the region.38

### **Comparative Positioning**

Relative to other North African exporters like Morocco and Mauritania, Egypt possesses a more advanced existing industrial base and the unique logistical advantage of the Suez Canal.12 However, Morocco is currently perceived as having a more "bankable" regulatory package ("Morocco’s Offer"), which bundles land and grid access more effectively than Egypt's current fragmented system.12

## **Political Economy Constraints and Implementation Risks**

Despite the momentum, several structural challenges threaten the scalability of Egypt’s hydrogen ambitions.

### **Financial Constraints**

The primary challenge is the "cost of capital." Developers in Egypt face borrowing rates of 16–18%, whereas OECD-based projects borrow at 4–6%.12 Even with extremely low renewable generation costs (approx. 2 cents/kWh), the high debt-servicing costs make Egyptian green hydrogen expensive on a delivered basis without significant concessional financing or blended finance solutions.12

### **Infrastructure and Regulatory Bottlenecks**

* **Grid Capacity:** The $5–$6 billion transmission upgrade required to unlock Egypt's 180 GW potential remains largely "on paper".12  
* **Wheeling Charges:** There is significant uncertainty regarding the "wheeling charges" (grid transport fees) that the EETC will apply to hydrogen projects, which directly impacts the project’s internal rate of return (IRR).12  
* **Land Access:** While vast tracts of land are allocated, the technical process of coordinating land permits across the NREA, the military, and local governorates remains complex.12

### **Demand Uncertainty**

The global market for green hydrogen is still characterized by a lack of binding offtake contracts.12 Without long-term (15–20 year) purchase agreements from European utilities or industrial users, most Egyptian mega-projects cannot reach FID.12

## **Credibility Assessment and Outlook**

The Egyptian hydrogen sector is currently one of the most advanced in North Africa in terms of policy architecture and pilot implementation, but it remains aspirational at the GW-scale.

### **Narrative vs. Implementation**

* **Advanced Policy/Institutional Building:** Egypt has successfully created a "Golden License" system, passed Law No. 2 of 2024, and established a National Council. This places it ahead of most emerging markets in terms of regulatory readiness.9  
* **Implementation Gap:** Outside of the 100 MW "Egypt Green" project, the vast majority of the pipeline is in the "MoU" or "Pre-FEED" stage. The credibility of the 2040 targets (5.6 million tons) depends entirely on the next five years of grid modernization and the successful execution of the first wave of GW-scale ammonia plants.4

### **The 5-to-10 Year Outlook**

The most credible pathway for the next decade is the **Decarbonization of Ammonia and Bunkering**. The integration of electrolysis into existing ammonia plants for the fertilizer export market and the supply of green methanol to Suez Canal ships are bankable today.18 In contrast, large-scale hydrogen pipelines to Europe or the full transition of the domestic steel industry to H-DRI are likely 10-to-15-year prospects contingent on massive external financing.4
