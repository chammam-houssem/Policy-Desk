# **The Jordanian Green Hydrogen Frontier: A Strategic Analysis of Policy, Market Dynamics, and Geopolitical Integration in the Levantine Energy Transition**

The Hashemite Kingdom of Jordan stands at a critical juncture in its economic and energy history. Traditionally categorized as a resource-constrained nation dependent on energy imports for over 90% of its needs, Jordan is aggressively pivoting toward a future where it serves as a regional and global hub for green hydrogen production and export. This transition is not merely a technical shift in the energy mix but a foundational pillar of the Economic Modernization Vision 2033, aiming to leverage the country’s world-class renewable energy resources and strategic geographic positioning to foster a new industrial era. The pursuit of a green hydrogen economy represents a sophisticated synthesis of climate mitigation, industrial decarbonization, and geopolitical realignment. By developing the infrastructure to produce, store, and export green molecules—specifically green hydrogen and its derivatives like green ammonia and e-methanol—Jordan seeks to decouple its economic growth from fossil fuel volatility while securing its place in the emerging global value chains of the European and Asian energy markets.1

## **Strategic Ambition and the National Policy Framework**

The strategic ambition of Jordan is formalised through a hierarchy of policy documents that provide a roadmap from current pilot phases to a fully integrated hydrogen economy by 2050\. At the apex of this framework is the National Green Hydrogen Strategy, which outlines a vision of Jordan as a leading Power-to-X (PtX) player in the Middle East and North Africa (MENA) region. This strategy is reinforced by the Updated Energy Strategy 2035, which targets a 40% share of renewables in electricity generation by 2035, up from approximately 31% in 2030, and the Economic Modernization Vision 2033, which identifies green energy as a primary driver of high-value industrial growth.1

The government’s ambition is quantified through three distinct production scenarios: base, medium, and high ambition. Under the base scenario, Jordan aims to produce 0.6 million metric tons per annum (MMTPA) of green hydrogen by 2030, with 0.5 MMTPA dedicated to the export market and 0.1 MMTPA for domestic industrial consumption. By 2040, the target scales to 1.5 MMTPA, eventually reaching 3.4 MMTPA by 2050\. These targets are supported by an exponential ramp-up in renewable energy capacity, necessitating 8 GW of solar and wind by 2030, 22 GW by 2040, and 47 GW by 2050\.1

The economic implications of these targets are staggering, with total investment needs estimated at USD 175 billion by 2050\. The mobilization of such capital requires not only domestic policy clarity but also the establishment of international de-risking mechanisms and climate finance partnerships. Jordan’s strategy focuses on five strategic priorities: strengthening measures to support hydrogen investments, stimulating local market demand, facilitating a low-cost supply chain through regulation and incentives, leveraging its existing renewable energy leadership, and creating an enabling environment through standards and workforce development.1

## **Legislative Scaffolding: Modernizing the Energy Regulatory Environment**

The transition from announced ambition to official policy is most evident in the legislative reforms undertaken in 2025 and 2026\. The Jordanian government has recognized that a competitive hydrogen economy cannot be built on an antiquated legal framework designed for traditional utilities. Consequently, the Ministry of Energy and Mineral Resources (MEMR), in cooperation with the World Bank and the European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD), has spearheaded the drafting and approval of several landmark laws.1

### **The 2025 Gas Law and the Integration of Hydrogen**

The 2025 Gas Law, which received Cabinet endorsement in August 2025 and subsequent legislative approval in early 2026, serves as the primary regulatory instrument for the gaseous fuel market. Crucially, the law updates previous definitions of gas to explicitly include green hydrogen, biogas, and biomethane. This legislative flexibility is designed to accommodate the global energy transition while providing a unified framework for the import, transportation, distribution, storage, and sale of these products.13

The Gas Law introduces a comprehensive licensing regime managed by the Energy and Minerals Regulatory Commission (EMRC). This includes:

* **Wholesale and Retail Licenses:** Establishing a transparent system for the commercialization of hydrogen and its derivatives, ensuring that both private developers and public utilities operate under clear fee structures and performance benchmarks.13  
* **Private Infrastructure Provisions:** Allowing private entities to design, build, and operate gas pipelines, which is a critical shift from the previous state-led monopoly. This enables hydrogen developers to construct dedicated transport networks connecting production sites to the Port of Aqaba.1  
* **Open Access Principles:** Mandating that shared transmission and distribution networks operate on a non-discriminatory basis, allowing multiple producers to utilize common-use infrastructure (CUI).13  
* **Article 11 and Land Rights:** The law includes provisions for the acquisition of private land for strategic energy projects based on public interest, a measure that has sparked debate regarding property rights but is deemed essential for the rapid build-out of 450-kilometer conveyance corridors.19

### **Electricity Law Amendments and the Decoupling of the National Grid**

Parallel to the Gas Law, amendments to the General Electricity Law in 2025 have fundamentally altered the relationship between renewable energy producers and the national grid. Previously, the National Electric Power Company (NEPCO) operated as a single buyer, a model that created bottlenecks and led to a moratorium on large-scale renewable projects between 2019 and 2022 due to grid stability concerns. The 2025 amendments remove these barriers by allowing renewable energy generators to sell power directly to green hydrogen producers.1

The new regulatory environment permits the creation of "independent power transmission systems" and "self-generation stations." This is a critical enabler for hydrogen production, as it allows developers to build massive solar and wind farms dedicated to electrolysis without overloading the existing national electricity grid. Furthermore, electricity storage—including both chemical batteries and pumped hydro—is now a licensable activity, providing a legal basis for the 24/7 baseload renewable power required for efficient electrolyzer operation.1

## **Resource Endowments: Solar, Wind, and the LCOH Advantage**

Jordan’s primary competitive advantage in the global green hydrogen market is the exceptional quality of its renewable energy resources. The country enjoys high solar radiation figures of 4 to 7 kWh/m² per day, with over 300 sunny days per year. Simultaneously, wind speeds in certain southern and eastern regions, such as Ma'an and Quweira, exceed 8.0 m/s, providing a complementary profile that maximizes the capacity factor of electrolyzers.1

| Resource Type | Technical Specification | Geographic Focus |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Solar Radiation | 4 – 7 kWh/m2 per day | Ma’an, Quweira, Risha |
| Wind Speed | \> 8.0 m/s at 100m height | Tafila, Ma’an, Aqaba |
| PV Power Potential | High Specific Yield | Southern Desert Region |
| Wind Capacity Factor | 35% \- 45% (Estimated) | High-altitude Ridges |

The Levelized Cost of Hydrogen (LCOH) is the fundamental metric determining Jordan’s export competitiveness. Research indicates that the lowest-cost hydrogen in Jordan can be achieved through a hybrid blend of solar and wind power, allowing electrolyzers to operate with a high load factor. While initial costs for pilot projects are estimated between $6.5 and $7.6 per kg of H2, the national strategy targets a reduction to $2.33/kg by 2030 and potentially $2.00/kg by 2050 as economies of scale and technological improvements in electrolyzers are realized.2

A critical component of this cost calculation is the "transmission penalty." Producing hydrogen at the source of the renewable resource and transporting the gas is generally more cost-effective than transporting electricity long distances to the production hub. For instance, expanding electricity transmission from the northern renewable zones to the southern production centers is estimated to increase the LCOH from 2.33 €/kg to 2.72 €/kg. This economic reality is driving the policy preference for a "hydrogen backbone" pipeline over traditional high-voltage overhead lines (OHTL) for the long-term expansion phases.2

## **The Water-Energy-Hydrogen Nexus: Desalination as an Enabler**

As one of the world's most water-scarce nations, with renewable freshwater availability well below the absolute scarcity threshold of 100 cubic meters per person per year, Jordan cannot divert existing water resources toward hydrogen production. The development of a green hydrogen economy is therefore inextricably linked to the kingdom’s broader water security strategy, specifically the Aqaba-Amman Water Desalination and Conveyance Project (AAWDCP).23

### **The AAWDCP and the National Carrier Project**

The AAWDCP, also known as the National Carrier Project, is a USD 6 billion infrastructure undertaking designed to supply 300 million cubic meters (MCM) of desalinated water annually from the Red Sea to Amman and other governorates. While the primary objective is to meet approximately 40% of the country’s municipal water demand by 2040, the project serves as the foundational marine infrastructure for the green hydrogen sector.25

| AAWDCP Component | Detail | Strategic Relevance for Hydrogen |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Seawater Intake | Located in Aqaba’s Industrial Port | Shared intake facilities for hydrogen plants |
| Desalination Method | Seawater Reverse Osmosis (SWRO) | Provides high-purity water for electrolysis |
| Conveyance System | 450 km underground pipeline | Potential shared corridors for gas/H2 |
| Captive Solar Plant | 281 MWp plant in Al-Quweira | Decoupled RE power model for large scale utilities |
| Brine Discharge | High-velocity discharge system | Shared outfall infrastructure for hub archetypes |

The cost of desalinated water is estimated at approximately $0.9/m³, which adds a marginal but manageable cost to the hydrogen production process. Because green hydrogen production via electrolysis requires approximately 9 liters of high-purity water per kilogram of H2, the water-related OPEX represents less than 2% of the total LCOH. This makes seawater desalination a viable and sustainable solution for Jordan’s hydrogen ambitions, provided the capital investment for common-use marine works is shared across multiple projects.6

## **Industrial Strategy: Domestic Demand and the PtX Value Chain**

Jordan’s industrial strategy for hydrogen is built on the concept of "Power-to-X," where green hydrogen serves as a versatile feedstock for various hard-to-abate sectors. The domestic market is centered on four primary pillars: fertilizers, refining, heavy industry, and heavy-duty transport. By stimulating domestic demand, the government aims to create a stable "base-load" offtake that de-risks larger export-oriented investments while simultaneously meeting its commitments under the Paris Agreement and the EU’s Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM).2

### **The Fertilizer Industry: A Strategic Offtaker**

The fertilizer industry is the most immediate and economically significant offtaker for green hydrogen. Jordan is a major global producer of phosphate and potash, but it currently imports approximately 350,000 tonnes of ammonia annually to produce finished fertilizers. Replacing these imports with domestically produced green ammonia would require roughly 1 GW of electrolyzer capacity and 62,000 tonnes of green hydrogen per year. This transition would not only enhance Jordan’s energy independence but also provide a "green premium" for its fertilizer exports to the European market, where buyers are increasingly demanding zero-carbon inputs.2

### **Refining and Heavy Industry**

The Jordan Petroleum Refinery Company (JPRC) and other industrial entities in sectors such as cement and steel manufacturing are major users of fossil-based hydrogen and industrial process heat. The "Preliminary Road Map for Green Hydrogen Usage in the Industry Sector for Heat Application," developed with technical assistance from the MED-GEM Network, identifies hydrogen blending as a transitional solution to decarbonize these processes. In the steel and cement sectors, which require temperatures exceeding 1000°C, hydrogen offers one of the few viable pathways for deep decarbonization where electrification is technically or economically unfeasible.1

| Domestic Sector | Potential Application | Hydrogen Demand (Estimated 2030\) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Fertilizers | Green Ammonia Synthesis | 62,000 tonnes / year |
| Refining | Hydrocracking & Desulfurization | 15,000 – 25,000 tonnes / year |
| Heavy Industry | Industrial Process Heat (Cement/Steel) | 10,000 tonnes / year |
| Transport | Heavy-duty Fuel Cell Vehicles | 5,000 tonnes / year |

### **The Role of PtX in National Decarbonization**

Beyond its role as a feedstock, green hydrogen is viewed as a strategic tool for energy storage and grid balancing. As renewable energy penetration exceeds 35% of total electricity generation, the intermittency of solar and wind resources will require large-scale storage solutions. Green hydrogen, stored in compressed tanks or converted back to power through fuel cells, can provide seasonal and long-duration storage that chemical batteries cannot currently achieve at a comparable cost. This "converged ecosystem" allows the hydrogen sector to support the stability of the national electricity grid while maximizing the utilization of the country's solar and wind resources.10

## **Infrastructure: The Aqaba Green Hydrogen Hub**

The realization of Jordan’s export ambitions depends on the development of common-use infrastructure (CUI) in the Aqaba Special Economic Zone (ASEZ). The MEMR, in coordination with the Aqaba Development Corporation (ADC) and the EBRD, is planning a centralized Green Hydrogen Hub that will serve as the gateway for Jordan’s hydrogen economy. This hub approach is designed to achieve economies of scale and reduce the specific CAPEX for individual developers.6

### **Layout and Hub Archetypes**

The planned hub is located on a 5.5 square kilometer land parcel within the vicinity of existing fertilizer plants and the industrial port. Detailed technical assessments have compared different development archetypes:

* **Common Hub Approach (Joint SPV):** Developers share electrolyzers and ammonia synthesis facilities. This approach yields the highest land-use efficiency and could support a production capacity of up to 15.6 MMTPA of green ammonia.6  
* **Integrated Production Facilities:** Individual developers set up separate, self-contained facilities. This approach is more restricted, with a projected capacity of 6 MMTPA, which matches the 2040 national export target.6

The estimated CAPEX for the hub’s common infrastructure to meet the 2030 target of 3 MMTPA of ammonia is approximately USD 11 billion. This includes the desalination plant, electricity transmission lines, ammonia synthesis units, and storage facilities.6

### **Energy and Ammonia Transmission**

One of the most significant infrastructure challenges is the transport of energy and finished products. The CUI masterplan evaluates two primary modes for energy transmission:

1. **Overhead Transmission Lines (OHTL):** A 420-kilometer, 400-kV AC grid connecting renewable zones to Aqaba.  
2. **Hydrogen Pipelines:** A 100-bar high-pressure gas system.

While OHTL is the conventional choice for immediate projects, technical analysis shows that as production scales, the specific CAPEX of a hydrogen pipeline becomes 28% lower than that of OHTL by 2050\. This is due to the lower energy losses and higher throughput capacity of gaseous transmission over long distances. For ammonia handling, the plan favors cooled liquid pipelines (at \-33°C) for short distances between the hub and the export berths to minimize operational complexity and prevent boil-off gas.6

### **Port Facilities and Export Terminals**

The existing berths at the Port of Aqaba are insufficient to handle the projected export volumes of 2030\. The infrastructure roadmap proposes the construction of a new dedicated berth at a cost of USD 65 million, capable of handling tankers up to 80,000 DWT (LR 1 category). This facility would be equipped for the specialized loading of green ammonia and methanol, integrating safety and origin-certification protocols required by international markets.4

As of late 2025, Jordan has signed 14 Memoranda of Understanding (MoUs) and one framework agreement with various international developers. These agreements commit investors to conducting preliminary feasibility studies and, in advanced cases, front-end engineering and design (FEED).20

### **Case Study: The Jordan Green Ammonia (JGA) Project**

The JGA project, a joint venture between Polish developer Hynfra and UAE-based Fidelity Group, is currently the "front-runner" in the Jordanian pipeline. In March 2026, JGA signed a FEED agreement with Danish technology leader Topsoe to utilize its ModuLite ammonia synthesis platform. The project aims to deploy 700 MW of renewable energy to produce 100,000 tonnes of green ammonia annually for export to international fertilizer markets. With a total projected investment of USD 1.6 billion, the JGA project has already moved into detailed engineering, with a final investment decision (FID) expected in 2027 and operation targeted for 2030\.37

### **The Retreat of Early Pioneers: The Fortescue Suspension**

A critical reality check for the sector was the suspension of all Middle Eastern projects by Australia’s Fortescue Future Industries (FFI) in July 2024\. FFI was the first foreign investor to sign a framework agreement with Jordan in 2021, but broader global industry retrenchment and the prioritization of domestic Australian projects led to a withdrawal. This event highlights the volatility of the global hydrogen market and the competition for limited capital and electrolyzer supply. Jordan’s response has been to diversify its partner base, with a notable increase in interest from Chinese state-owned and private firms such as CTGI and HDsolar, which bring massive manufacturing capabilities and different financing structures to the table.20

## **Geopolitical Positioning: Jordan as a Green Energy Bridge**

Jordan’s hydrogen strategy is fundamentally an exercise in geopolitical repositioning. By developing a green hydrogen economy, the kingdom seeks to transition from a vulnerable energy importer to a strategic "Green Energy Bridge" between the resource-rich Arabian Peninsula and the high-demand markets of the European Union. This positioning is supported by three primary geopolitical pillars: the EU-Jordan Green Deal Partnership, regional cooperation through the Arab Gas Pipeline, and the utilization of H2Global and other climate finance mechanisms.4

### **The EU-Jordan Partnership and CBAM**

The European Union’s REPowerEU plan, which targets 10 million tonnes of green hydrogen imports by 2030, identifies the Southern Neighborhood as a critical supply region. Jordan’s proximity to Europe via the Suez Canal and its participation in the EU-Jordan Green Deal make it an attractive partner. Furthermore, the advent of the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) provides a powerful economic incentive for Jordan to decarbonize its industrial exports (fertilizers, chemicals, and cement) to maintain its access to the EU market. By aligning its national certification schemes with the EU’s RFNBO rules, Jordan ensures that its hydrogen and ammonia carry the necessary "green credentials" for premium European pricing.2

### **Regional Competition and Cooperation**

In the regional landscape, Jordan must navigate its relationship with giants like Saudi Arabia (NEOM project), Egypt, and Morocco. While these countries have larger land masses and, in some cases, existing sovereign wealth funds to drive development, Jordan’s advantage lies in its regulatory agility and political stability. Jordan is actively exploring regional cooperation, such as blending hydrogen into the Arab Gas Pipeline (AGP) for transit to Israel and Europe. Research shows that blending between 5% and 20% hydrogen into the AGP is technically feasible without major infrastructure modifications, offering a low-cost transitional export route.2

| Egypt | Suez Canal, existing LNG/Ammonia infra | Large-scale bunkering & maritime export |

### **Financing the Transition: H2Global and Green Bonds**

The financing of Jordan’s hydrogen economy requires sophisticated blended finance models. The government is leveraging the German-led H2Global mechanism, a double-auction model that provides federal grants to offset the difference between the high purchase price of green hydrogen and the lower selling price in the European market. While the first H2Global auction was awarded to an Egyptian project, Jordan is positioning itself for the second EUR 3.5 billion funding window. Additionally, Jordan’s Sovereign Green Bond Framework, established in 2025, aims to mobilize private capital for "common-use" infrastructure, diversifying the investor base and signaling fiscal commitment to the energy transition.43

## **Economic and Technical Challenges: A Nuanced Risk Assessment**

Despite the robust policy and legislative progress, Jordan’s hydrogen ambitions face several systemic risks that could impact the timeline and scale of deployment. These include technical grid constraints, the high capital cost of technology, and the "chicken-and-egg" problem of market off-take.

### **Grid Integration and Storage**

The rapid expansion of renewable capacity (8 GW by 2030\) presents significant challenges for the National Electric Power Company (NEPCO). The Jordanian grid is currently relatively small and lacks the flexibility to manage massive amounts of intermittent power without substantial investment in smart-grid technology and storage. While the 2025 Electricity Law permits independent lines for hydrogen plants, any connection to the main grid will require sophisticated "smart-meter" and "time-of-use" tariff structures to manage peak loads and ensure system reliability. Without these upgrades, the curtailment of renewable power could undermine the economic viability of electrolyzer projects.1

### **The Cost of Technology and Supply Chain Risks**

Electrolyzers remain high-CAPEX items, with costs ranging from USD 500 to USD 1,400 per kW. Jordan’s reliance on imported technology makes its hydrogen projects vulnerable to global supply chain disruptions and currency fluctuations. Furthermore, the "learning curve" for electrolyzer efficiency is still ongoing, and early adopters risk investing in technology that may become obsolete within a decade. The national strategy’s plan to localize electrolyzer manufacturing by the 2030s is a response to this risk, but it requires a level of technical capability and industrial base that Jordan is currently still building.1

### **Off-take and Market Price Volatility**

The most significant bottleneck for Jordan’s 14 MoUs is the lack of binding, long-term off-take agreements. Most potential buyers in Europe are still in the process of defining their own procurement strategies and are hesitant to lock in prices that are significantly higher than grey hydrogen. This "off-take gap" makes it difficult for developers to secure project financing and reach a Final Investment Decision (FID). Jordan is attempting to mitigate this by focusing on the domestic fertilizer market as a "captive" buyer, but the domestic market alone cannot support the USD 175 billion in investment needed for the 2050 vision.2

## **Realistic Near- to Medium-Term Deployment Prospects**

Based on the current project pipeline and infrastructure development, the deployment of hydrogen in Jordan is likely to occur in three distinct phases: the Pilot Phase (2025-2030), the Scaling Phase (2030-2040), and the Hub Phase (2040-2050).

### **The Pilot Phase (2025-2030): Establishing the Proof of Concept**

In the near term, realistic deployment will be limited to small-to-medium scale pilot projects focused on "low-hanging fruit." The JGA project ($1.6 billion, 100k t/y ammonia) is the most likely to reach operation by 2030\. During this period, the primary focus will be on the construction of the AAWDCP desalination project and the initial common-use facilities in Aqaba. The government will also need to finalize all implementing bylaws for the Gas and Electricity Laws and establish a national hydrogen certification unit. Success in this phase is measured not by volume but by the creation of a "bankable" project structure that attracts international lenders.1

### **The Scaling Phase (2030-2040): Industrial Transformation**

If the pilots prove successful and the AAWDCP is operational, the medium-term will see the scale-up of production toward the 1.5 MMTPA target. This phase will be driven by the full conversion of Jordan’s fertilizer industry to green ammonia and the initial integration of hydrogen into the refining and heavy industry sectors. The first dedicated hydrogen export pipelines to Europe or regional neighbors may begin construction during this decade. This phase is highly dependent on global market conditions and the narrowing of the green-grey price gap.1

### **The Hub Phase (2040-2050): Global Leadership**

The long-term vision of 3.4 MMTPA and USD 175 billion in investment represents a "best-case" scenario where Jordan is a fully integrated hub. Achieving this requires the complete transformation of the Levantine energy landscape and the establishment of liquid hydrogen or ammonia corridors. At this stage, hydrogen becomes a primary export commodity for Jordan, comparable to the role of natural gas in neighboring economies.1

## **Strategic Synthesis and Expert Conclusions**

The Jordan green hydrogen economy is currently in the "Appraise and Prepare" stage. The transition from ambition to reality is being managed with a high degree of technical and regulatory sophistication, as evidenced by the concurrent modernization of the Gas and Electricity Laws and the integration of hydrogen into the national water strategy. Jordan has correctly identified that in a global market where multiple regions have abundant sun and wind, the differentiating factors are regulatory certainty, infrastructure readiness, and political stability.6

**Key Analytical Findings:**

1. **Legislative Advantage:** Jordan’s 2025 Gas Law is one of the most comprehensive in the region, providing a clear legal definition for hydrogen and a mechanism for private infrastructure ownership. This significantly de-risks the regulatory environment for foreign direct investment.13  
2. **Infrastructure Linkage:** The linkage between the USD 6 billion AAWDCP desalination project and the hydrogen sector is a masterstroke of multi-sectoral planning. It solves the water scarcity constraint while providing the marine works necessary for industrial-scale electrolysis.6  
3. **Market Realism:** The withdrawal of FFI and the "structural slowdown" noted by technology leaders like Topsoe suggest that Jordan's timeline may be pushed back. A realistic expectation is that the 2030 targets will be met primarily by domestic industrial offtake and smaller-scale export pilots, with the massive million-ton exports materializing closer to 2040\.20  
4. **The China Pivot:** The increasing presence of Chinese developers (CTGI, HDsolar, UEG) in the project pipeline indicates a shift in the investment landscape. Jordan is successfully positioning itself as an open and attractive market for both Western and Eastern capital, balancing its geopolitical relationships to secure its energy future.36

In conclusion, Jordan is positioning itself not just as a producer of clean energy, but as a sophisticated regulatory and industrial platform for the global energy transition. While significant technical and financial hurdles remain, the strategic alignment of the kingdom’s leadership, the modernization of its laws, and the pragmatic focus on shared infrastructure suggest that Jordan will be a pivotal player in the 21st-century green molecule economy.1
