# **The Hydrogen Sovereign: A Strategic Analysis of Morocco’s Green Molecule Industrialization and Geopolitical Pivot**

The Kingdom of Morocco is currently navigating a structural transformation of its national political economy, shifting from a historical position as a resource-constrained importer of fossil fuels toward a future as a vertically integrated producer and exporter of green molecules. This transition is not merely a component of environmental policy but is the central pillar of a new Moroccan industrial doctrine and a strategic tool for geopolitical repositioning within the Euro-Mediterranean and Atlantic spheres. Morocco’s traditional energy vulnerability, characterized by a 90% dependence on imported hydrocarbons, was sharply highlighted by the geopolitical rupture of 2021, which saw the cessation of Algerian natural gas flows via the Maghreb-Europe Gas (GME) pipeline.1 This event catalyzed a shift from a reactive energy security posture to an assertive strategy of "energy sovereignty," centered on the exploitation of the country’s unique wind and solar complementarity to produce cost-competitive green hydrogen (![][image1]) and its derivatives, notably green ammonia (![][image2]) and synthetic fuels.3

## **The Strategic Imperative: Decoupling and Sovereignty**

The impetus for Morocco’s hydrogen ambitions resides in the convergence of three critical factors: the necessity of industrial decarbonization for the phosphate sector, the urgent need to stabilize the trade balance by substituting ammonia imports, and the strategic opportunity to serve as a near-shore energy hub for a European Union under the pressure of the REPowerEU mandate.4 The Moroccan state views green hydrogen as a "game changer" capable of driving sustainable industrialization while reinforcing the country's relevance as a trans-Mediterranean energy partner.4

Historically, Morocco’s energy landscape was dominated by coal and imported oil. However, the 2009 National Energy Strategy set a trajectory for renewables that reached approximately 37% of installed capacity by 2020 and nearly 45% by 2025, with an ultimate target of 52% by 2030\.3 The development of green hydrogen is the logical evolution of this path, moving from the generation of green electrons for domestic power to the production of green molecules for industrial feedstock and global trade.7 This shift is facilitated by the country's exceptional renewable potential, where the combination of high solar irradiation and persistent Atlantic trade winds allows for electrolyzer utilization factors that are significantly higher than those achievable in most European latitudes.5

The transition is underscored by a sophisticated energy trilemma: balancing the drive for economic growth with environmental commitments and the practicalities of a water-stressed geography. While the ambitious Green Hydrogen Roadmap (GHR) aims for a 10 TWh export market by 2030, economic modeling indicates that a singular focus on exports could inadvertently increase domestic emissions if the build-out of renewable capacity does not outpace the energy-intensive requirements of electrolysis and associated desalination.13 Consequently, the official policy has evolved to prioritize integrated "Power-to-X" (PtX) ecosystems that serve both domestic industrial needs and international markets.4

## **Official Policy and Institutional Architecture**

The Moroccan policy framework for hydrogen is distinguished by its transition from high-level "announced ambition" to "operational reality" through a sequence of strategic documents and institutional reforms. The foundational Green Hydrogen Roadmap, unveiled in 2021, established the initial vision of a three-phase development cycle aimed at short-term industrial use, medium-term transport integration, and long-term cross-sectoral maturity.5

### **The "Offre Maroc" (Morocco Offer)**

In March 2024, the government operationalized this vision through the "Offre Maroc," a comprehensive framework designed to simplify the investor pathway and provide clear fiscal and land-use incentives.12 Unlike previous iterations of energy policy, the Morocco Offer provides a "single focal point" through the Moroccan Agency for Sustainable Energy (MASEN), which coordinates the entire value chain from renewable generation to the downstream production of ammonia and methanol.12

| Governance | Steering Committee | Chaired by the Head of Government; MASEN as single window 15 |
| Fiscal Support | Investment Charter | VAT and Customs exemptions; Sectoral and geographic bonuses 14 |
| Infrastructure | Shared Assets | State commitment to provide desalination, port, and grid connectivity 12 |

The Offer applies to "integrated projects" that encompass upstream renewable power, electrolysis, and downstream transformation.12 By June 2025, MASEN had received over 40 project proposals from international consortiums, reflecting the high bankability of the Moroccan regulatory environment.4 The institutional structure is further supported by the National Hydrogen Commission (established in 2019\) and the Green H2 Morocco cluster, which foster R\&D and local industrial integration.5

### **The Institutional Nexus: MASEN, ONEE, and ONHYM**

The success of the policy depends on a tripartite institutional arrangement. MASEN manages the renewable and hydrogen projects, while the National Office of Electricity and Drinking Water (ONEE) oversees the transmission infrastructure required to evacuate power from the wind-rich southern provinces to the industrial north.14 Simultaneously, the National Office of Hydrocarbons and Mines (ONHYM) has pivoted to explore "white" or natural hydrogen, identifying eight areas of interest where surface seepages suggest the presence of significant underground deposits.5 This multi-agency approach ensures that the hydrogen strategy is not an isolated energy project but a core component of the national development plan.14

## **Industrial Strategy: The OCP Anchor and Fertilizer Autonomy**

The most significant driver of the domestic hydrogen market is the OCP Group (Office Chérifien des Phosphates), the state-owned global leader in phosphate fertilizers. OCP’s industrial strategy is the primary engine of Morocco's hydrogen deployment, as the company seeks to decouple its production from the volatility of international natural gas markets. In 2022, OCP spent approximately $2 billion on importing "grey" ammonia—derived from natural gas—making it the world’s largest importer of the commodity.20

### **The Green Investment Program (2023–2027)**

OCP has launched a $13 billion "Green Investment Program" to achieve 100% carbon neutrality by 2040, with a target of 100% renewable power by 2027\.22 The program’s centerpiece is the massive scale-up of green ammonia production to achieve "ammonia autonomy" and secure its position in a global market increasingly governed by carbon pricing and mechanisms like the EU’s Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM).21

The economics of this pivot are increasingly favorable. While green ammonia was estimated at $700 per tonne in 2022 compared to $300–$600 for grey ammonia, the gap is narrowing as renewable costs plummet and natural gas prices remain volatile.21 OCP forecasts that domestically produced green ammonia will act as a strategic hedge, with costs falling to $400–$450 per tonne by 2035\.21 This transition also allows OCP to pioneer "green fertilizers" tailored to the specific soil needs of the African continent, where it already maps 30 million hectares of soil to optimize nutrient delivery.20

### **The Water-Energy-Food Nexus**

A critical challenge for OCP and the broader Moroccan hydrogen sector is the requirement for high-purity water for electrolysis in one of the world's most water-stressed regions.1 OCP’s strategy involves a total reliance on "non-conventional water" sources. By 2027, the company aims to cover 100% of its industrial water needs through desalination powered by renewable energy.20 The OCP Green Water project, supported by a €100 million loan from the International Finance Corporation (IFC), serves as a model for large-scale desalination integration, supplying both industrial facilities and neighboring urban populations.27 This approach mitigates the risk of social friction by ensuring that hydrogen production does not compete with agricultural or domestic water needs.20

## **The Project Pipeline and Deployment Prospects**

Morocco’s project pipeline has moved beyond Memoranda of Understanding (MoUs) into the phase of land reservation and Front-End Engineering Design (FEED). As of March 2025, the government has selected five national and international consortiums for six major projects totaling $32.8 billion in investment.5

### **Tier 1: The "Chbika" and Integrated Export Projects**

The "Chbika" project is currently the vanguard of Morocco’s export-oriented hydrogen sector. Developed by TE H2 (80% TotalEnergies, 20% EREN) alongside Copenhagen Infrastructure Partners (CIP) and A.P. Moller Capital, it is located in the Guelmim-Oued Noun region.29 The first phase aims for 1 GW of onshore wind and solar capacity to produce 200,000 tonnes of green ammonia per year for the European market.30 The project reached a "preliminary land reservation" milestone in October 2024 and is currently undergoing pre-FEED studies, with construction activities projected to ramp up by 2026\.30

### **Tier 2: Mid-Scale and Domestic Innovation**

Beyond the mega-projects, a series of mid-scale and pilot initiatives are testing technology integration. The "Power-to-X" project in collaboration with the German government targets 10,000 tonnes of hydrogen annually from a 100 MW electrolyser system.7 Additionally, the GAIA Energy and H2PRO partnership is exploring E-TAC (Electrochemical, Thermally Activated Chemical) electrolysis technology with an initial 10–20 MW capacity.7 These projects serve as essential "Proof of Concept" platforms, capitalizing on the GreenH2A research facility to refine production pathways before utility-scale deployment.5

### **Realistic Deployment Outlook (2025–2030)**

While the announced ambitions are vast, the realistic near-term prospects suggest a gradual ramp-up. The first industrial-scale volumes of green ammonia are expected from OCP’s Jorf Lasfar facility by late 2026, followed by the first phase of the Tarfaya project in 2027\.20 Utility-scale export projects like Chbika are more likely to reach full operational status toward 2028–2030, pending final investment decisions (FIDs) and the resolution of the "contracting gap" in the European market.21

## **Infrastructure: The Arteries of the Hydrogen Economy**

The physical geography of Morocco presents a mismatch between the supply of renewable energy (concentrated in the South) and the demand for energy and industrial feedstock (concentrated in the North). To bridge this gap, the state is investing in a series of "Electric Highways" and port-linked hydrogen corridors.

### **The HVDC "Electric Highway" and Grid Expansion**

The ONEE is spearheading the construction of a 1,400-km High-Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) transmission line between Dakhla and Casablanca.19 This $3 billion project, being implemented via a PPP with a consortium including Nareva and Taqa Morocco, aims to evacuate up to 3,000 MW of renewable power.19 A parallel 1,000-km ultra-high-voltage (UHT) AC link with 2,000 MW capacity is also planned for commissioning by December 2028 to reinforce the national grid.35 These corridors are essential for both domestic energy security and the power-intensive processes of electrolysis in the northern industrial zones.19

### **Moroccan Ports as Green Hubs**

Morocco’s port strategy is a "triple-role" facilitator: acting as energy export hubs, industrial hubs, and providers of marine bunker fuels.28 The World Bank’s 2025 assessment identifies specific roles for key ports in the integrated hydrogen value chain.

| Port | Strategic Role | Potential / Constraints |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Tanger Med | Global Bunkering Hub | Leading potential for green methanol/ammonia; spatial constraints for local production 28 |
| Mohammedia | Storage & Energy Hub | Proximity to salt caverns (500 tons H2 each) makes it ideal for large-scale storage 28 |
| Nador West Med | Strategic Export | New port designed to handle high-volume energy exports and LNG/H2 mix 8 |
| Dakhla Atlantique | Integrated Frontier | Deepwater port being built to handle southern Sahara energy and ammonia exports 37 |

The reactivation of the GME pipeline in reverse mode (importing gas from Spain) has provided a temporary solution to the gas shortage, but the long-term plan envisions repurposing this infrastructure for the export of hydrogen to Europe.2 The European Hydrogen Backbone (EHB) initiative notes that transporting hydrogen via repurposed pipelines would be significantly more cost-effective (€0.11–0.21/kg per 1,000 km) than maritime shipping, making the GME a critical asset for future trade.40

## **The White Hydrogen Frontier: Natural Hydrogen Exploration**

A nascent but potentially disruptive element of the Moroccan strategy is the exploration of natural or "white" hydrogen. Unlike green hydrogen, which requires significant energy input, natural hydrogen is produced by geochemical processes in the Earth's crust.41 ONHYM has identified two areas with significant surface infiltrations and is conducting mapping and modeling to assess the underground potential.5

In 2025, the **HyMaroc** joint venture (Sound Energy and Getech Group) completed a territory-wide screening using a proprietary magnetic and gravity database and machine learning algorithms.42 The study identified multiple favorable areas across the Kingdom. While in the "early stages," the discovery of commercially viable natural hydrogen would drastically alter the cost curve of the national strategy, as exploration and production costs are theoretically lower than those of electrolysis-based pathways.41

## **Geopolitical Positioning and the Western Sahara Factor**

Morocco’s hydrogen strategy is inextricably linked to its geopolitical positioning and its "Atlantic orientation." The Kingdom has leveraged its energy transition to deepen ties with the European Union, specifically through the first-ever "Green Partnership" signed in 2022\.43 This partnership aligns Moroccan production with EU decarbonization targets, particularly the REPowerEU goal of importing 10 million tonnes of green hydrogen by 2030\.4

### **The Western Sahara Dilemma**

A significant portion of Morocco’s renewable and hydrogen potential is located in Western Sahara, a territory recognized by the UN as non-self-governing.10 Conservative estimates suggest that by 2030, wind capacity in the southern provinces could constitute nearly 50% of the national total.10

| Risk Category | Context / Impact | Evidence |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Legal Risk | ECJ Rulings on Origin | The European Court of Justice has previously annulled trade deals due to lack of Saharawi consent 10 |
| Political Risk | "Infrastructural Annexion" | Critics argue that HVDC lines and major wind farms entangle the territory irrevocably with the Moroccan state 35 |
| Ethical / ESG | Offtaker Resistance | Major European utilities may face internal ESG pressure to avoid molecules produced in disputed territories 10 |
| Regional Tension | Relations with Algeria | Tensions remain high, with the GME pipeline closure being a direct result of diplomatic rupture 1 |

Morocco has successfully navigated these risks by securing high-level diplomatic support, most notably from France, Germany, and Spain.22 The 2024 French recognition of Moroccan sovereignty over the territory was immediately followed by investment pledges from the French Development Agency (AFD) for projects in Laâyoune and Dakhla.22 However, the requirement for "explicit consent" of the Saharawi people remains a potential legal bottleneck for European companies like Enel, Siemens Energy, and Engie that are active in the region.10

### **South-South Cooperation and the African Hub**

Morocco is also positioning itself as a regional energy leader through South-South cooperation. The Nigeria-Morocco Gas Pipeline (NMGP) is a multi-billion-dollar project designed to link 13 West African countries to Europe.46 This pipeline is projected not only as a gas route but as a future corridor for hydrogen and clean energy trade, integrating West Africa into a Euro-African energy hub.33 Furthermore, OCP’s expansion into Nigeria and Ethiopia through green fertilizer plants underscores Morocco’s use of hydrogen as a tool of "fertilizer diplomacy" across the continent.20

## **Market Dynamics and Economic Reality Check**

Despite the robust policy support and large project pipeline, the Moroccan hydrogen sector faces several near-term economic hurdles. The "energy trilemma" remains acute: balancing the massive capital expenditure (CAPEX) requirements with the goal of providing affordable energy to the domestic market.

### **Cost Curves and Bankability**

Production costs in Morocco are among the most competitive in the MENA region, with estimates of €2–2.50 per kg of hydrogen by 2030, falling to as low as €1.00 per kg by 2050\.11 However, the current "contracting gap" is a major obstacle to FIDs. Potential offtakers in Europe are hesitant to sign long-term take-or-pay contracts at current prices, expecting that technological advancements and scale will halve costs over the next decade.21

The role of international financing is crucial. Programs like the H2Global initiative and the EU’s Green Energy funding (€165 million) are beginning to provide the "anchor" support needed to bridge the price gap between green and grey molecules.44 The PtX Development Fund’s €30 million grant to OCP’s Jorf Lasfar project is a prime example of how targeted international capital is de-risking the first generation of Moroccan projects.24

### **The Execution Gap**

A critical "execution gap" remains between announced capacity and commissioned assets. While the regional project pipeline in the MENA region has surged to 202 GW, the mobilization of capital and supply chain resilience are persistent challenges.49 In Morocco, the suspension of gas tenders in early 2026 suggests a period of "strategic reassessment" as the government aligns its traditional energy infrastructure with the evolving hydrogen roadmap.8 The ability to translate the 40+ project proposals into operating facilities will depend on the speed of infrastructure delivery, particularly the HVDC Electric Highways and desalination plants.4

## **Conclusions: Synthesis and Strategic Outlook**

Morocco’s hydrogen strategy is a sophisticated exercise in national self-assertion. It marks the transition from a passive energy consumer to an active industrial architect of the green energy transition. By anchoring the domestic market in the OCP Group's fertilizer production, Morocco has created a "first-mover advantage" that de-risks the capital-intensive build-out of renewable and electrolysis assets.

The "Offre Maroc" provides one of the most structured regulatory environments in the MENA region, but its success is tethered to the resolution of structural bottlenecks. The near-term focus (2025–2030) will likely center on domestic ammonia substitution and pilot export projects like Chbika. The medium-to-long-term (2030–2050) realization of the Kingdom’s 4% global market share goal will require the completion of massive infrastructure projects—notably the HVDC corridors and port-to-pipeline interconnections with Europe.

Geopolitically, Morocco has successfully positioned itself as an indispensable "green partner" for Europe, effectively using its energy transition to secure diplomatic recognition of its claims in Western Sahara. However, this legal "frontline" remains the primary external risk to the sector's long-term export ambitions. The emerging frontier of natural hydrogen exploration offers a potential paradigm shift that could further enhance Morocco's competitiveness.

Ultimately, the Moroccan hydrogen economy is not a generic energy project but a core element of a new national identity—a "Hydro-monarchy" seeking to combine its renewable wealth with advanced industrial processing to secure a dominant position in the post-carbon world. The next five years will be the decisive "proof of concept" phase, as the first mega-projects move from FEED to FID and finally to first production.
