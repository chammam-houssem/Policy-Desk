# Tunisia's hydrogen ambitions: bold targets, fragile foundations

**Tunisia has positioned itself as a future green hydrogen exporter to Europe, but a deep gap separates its strategic narrative from implementation reality.** The country's National Strategy for Green Hydrogen, published in 2024, envisions producing **8.3 million tonnes of hydrogen and derivatives annually by 2050** — roughly 72% for export to Europe via the SoutH2 Corridor pipeline. Yet as of early 2026, Tunisia produces zero green hydrogen, has no operational electrolysers, no hydrogen-specific legislation, and all announced large-scale projects remain at the memorandum of understanding stage. The strategy was developed principally by Germany's GIZ, reflects EU import priorities, and depends on approximately **€120 billion in investment** — more than double Tunisia's entire GDP — flowing into a country rated CCC+ by Fitch. The most credible near-term pathway is a modest pilot at Gabès producing 220 tonnes of hydrogen per year for domestic fertiliser use. Large-scale export production before the mid-2030s appears implausible based on expert assessments and the country's track record on renewable energy deployment.

---

## Tunisia's strategic hydrogen positioning

Tunisia frames hydrogen through multiple, sometimes competing, lenses — but the dominant framing is **export opportunity to Europe**. The Ministry of Industry, Mines and Energy (MIME), the institutional owner of the strategy, has consistently emphasised Tunisia's potential to become a net energy exporter after decades of growing energy dependence. With an energy deficit reaching approximately **50% in 2022–2023** and 97.5% of electricity generated from natural gas (nearly half imported from Algeria), the appeal of a hydrogen-powered reversal of fortune is politically powerful.

The strategy also invokes climate commitments (Tunisia's NDC targets a 45% carbon intensity reduction by 2030 versus 2010 levels), trade balance improvement (projected at €8.6 billion per year by 2050), and job creation (**430,000 jobs by 2050** per government projections). Southern Tunisia, historically marginalised and the site of periodic social unrest, is cast as the primary production zone, adding a regional development dimension.

The lead institutions are MIME, which chairs a dedicated Green Hydrogen Steering Committee (COPIL), and ANME (Agence Nationale pour la Maîtrise de l'Énergie), which implements energy policy. A task force under MIME handles negotiations with international partners. A National Observatory for Green Hydrogen value chains was planned but its operational status remains unclear. Key state-owned enterprises include **STEG** (the electricity and gas monopoly utility), **GCT** (Groupe Chimique Tunisien, the primary potential domestic offtaker), **ETAP** (state oil company), and **STIR** (refining). Germany's GIZ, which funded and shaped the strategy through the **H2Vert.TUN project** (€6 million, 2022–2025), remains the most influential external actor.

Critical voices — from the Transnational Institute, the Arab Reform Initiative, Tunisian civil society coalitions, and researchers at the Peace Research Institute Frankfurt — argue the strategy is **EU-driven rather than domestically owned**, designed primarily to serve European energy security while concentrating value-added processing in recipient countries. GIZ's own 2021 Power-to-X study acknowledged that if green hydrogen is "produced using imported technologies and exported as raw material, with further processing steps located in recipient countries, this would create limited jobs and value in Tunisia."

## Official plans, roadmaps, and targets

Tunisia's hydrogen ambitions rest on a single core document: the **"National Strategy for the Development of Green Hydrogen and Its Derivatives in Tunisia,"** first presented at a stakeholder dialogue on 4 October 2023 and published in summary form (English and French) in July 2024. It was developed by consulting firms GFA/ALCOR under GIZ commission for MIME.

The strategy sets out scenario-based production targets:

| Year | Total H₂ production | Domestic use | Export |
|------|---------------------|-------------|--------|
| 2030 | 320 kt/yr | 20 kt | 300 kt |
| 2035 | 1,140 kt/yr | 140 kt | 1,000 kt |
| 2040 | 2,110 kt/yr | 480 kt | 1,630 kt |
| 2050 | 8,300 kt/yr | ~1,900 kt | ~6,400 kt |

Supporting these targets, the strategy calls for approximately **100 GW of dedicated renewable energy capacity** by 2050 — roughly 15 times Tunisia's current total installed electricity generation capacity of ~6 GW. Over **500,000 hectares** (~3% of national territory) would be needed for renewable installations. Total cumulative investment is estimated at **~€120 billion**, with pipeline infrastructure alone costing €9.6–13.4 billion.

**These targets are not legally binding.** The strategy document explicitly states that "the feasibility of implementing the roadmap depends on international financial and organizational support and the signing of long-term framework agreements with off-takers, producers, partners and developers." There is no hydrogen law, no binding production mandates, and no legislated export quotas. The targets are best characterised as **strategic aspirations derived from feasibility-study scenarios**, not policy commitments backed by implementation mechanisms.

Tunisia's broader energy policy context includes a **35% renewable electricity target by 2030** (under the Tunisian Solar Plan), an energy sector strategy targeting 8,530 MW of renewables by 2035, and a National Low-Carbon Strategy aspiring to carbon neutrality by 2050. The hydrogen strategy sits alongside but is not formally integrated into these frameworks through binding legislation.

## Green hydrogen versus other pathways

Tunisia's strategy is **exclusively focused on green hydrogen** — electrolytic hydrogen produced from renewable electricity. The formal title specifies "green hydrogen and its derivatives," and the PtX (Power-to-X) framing is used extensively. The GIZ feasibility study that preceded the strategy was titled "Study on the Opportunities of Power-to-X in Tunisia."

**Blue hydrogen plays no role** in Tunisia's plans, which is logical given the country's structural position: Tunisia is a net gas importer (58% of natural gas comes from Algeria), has declining domestic gas production, possesses no CCS infrastructure, and has no characterised geological storage sites. Unlike Algeria, which could theoretically pursue blue hydrogen from its vast gas reserves, Tunisia has no feedstock basis for this pathway.

The primary derivative pathway is **green ammonia**, driven by the concrete industrial logic of replacing imported ammonia for GCT's fertiliser operations. Secondary pathways identified in the strategy include methanol, synthetic kerosene, and maritime fuels, though these remain speculative. The Gabès pilot project specifically targets the hydrogen-to-ammonia-to-fertiliser value chain.

Tunisia's definition of green hydrogen is being aligned with **EU delegated acts on renewable hydrogen**, reflecting the export orientation. The PtX Hub (a GIZ-supported platform) has provided capacity building on certification and guarantees of origin, though Tunisia currently lacks a domestic guarantees-of-origin system.

## Domestic demand and sectoral use cases

Tunisia's domestic hydrogen demand is **real but modest**, concentrated almost entirely in the fertiliser sector. GCT, the state-owned chemical group, processes approximately 6.5 million tonnes of phosphate rock annually to produce phosphoric acid, DAP, and MAP fertilisers. The company currently **imports all of its ammonia** — approximately **105,000 tonnes per year** — from Spain and Russia. Converting this to hydrogen-equivalent demand implies roughly **19,000 tonnes of H₂ per year**, a fraction of the strategy's 2050 target.

GCT's operations face growing external pressure from the EU's **Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)**, which threatens the competitiveness of Tunisian fertiliser exports to Europe unless production decarbonises. This creates a genuine, policy-driven incentive to switch from grey to green ammonia — the single most credible domestic demand driver.

Beyond fertilisers, domestic demand potential is thin. Tunisia's sole refinery (STIR at Bizerte, 34,000 barrels per day) uses limited hydrogen volumes. The country has **no significant steel production** requiring hydrogen-based direct reduction, no domestic methanol production, and a GIZ assessment found the "Tunisian industrial landscape is not currently adapted to benefit from green hydrogen" in sectors such as transport and heavy industry. Potential applications in metallurgical, ceramic, and glass sectors were identified by UNIDO but remain speculative and long-term.

The strategy allocates ~28% of 2050 production to domestic use, but the gap between current industrial demand (~19 kt H₂ equivalent) and the 1,900 kt domestic target is vast. The strategy is **overwhelmingly export-led**, and domestic use appears to function more as a political legitimacy mechanism than a primary demand driver.

## Project pipeline and implementation status

Tunisia signed a wave of hydrogen MoUs in 2024, creating an impressive-looking project pipeline on paper. In reality, **every project remains at the MoU or pre-feasibility stage**. No final investment decision has been taken, no construction has begun, and no electrolyser capacity is operational.

The most significant announced projects are:

**H2 Notos** (MoU signed May 2024) is the flagship, developed by TE H2 (an 80/20 joint venture of TotalEnergies and EREN Groupe) with Austria's Verbund. It targets 200,000 tonnes per year initially, scaling to 1 million tonnes, powered by solar and wind in southern Tunisia with desalinated water. The target market is Central Europe via the SoutH2 Corridor. Status: feasibility study phase; no FID, no construction timeline.

**ACWA Power** (MoU signed May 2024) proposes a three-phase buildout: 4 GW renewables and 2 GW electrolysers producing 200,000 tonnes per year initially, scaling to 12 GW renewables and 600,000 tonnes per year. ACWA suggested FID within 2–3 years of signing (i.e., 2026–2027), with commissioning around 2031. Status: pre-feasibility only.

**Amarenco/H2 Global Energy** (MoU signed July 2024) plans a $6 billion green hydrogen and ammonia hub at Gabès with 1.5–1.8 GW electrolysers producing 180,000 tonnes per year. Construction was targeted from 2028, commissioning by 2031. Status: pre-feasibility; the $6 billion figure is aspirational.

**HDF Energy** (MoU signed July 2024) proposes a €3 billion offshore-linked project combining 1 GW wind, 500 MW solar, and 800 MW electrolysers to produce 65,000 tonnes per year. Status: MoU only.

Four additional MoUs were signed on 29 July 2024 with **TuNur/Aker Horizons/Verbund**, **DEME HyPort**, **ABO Energy**, and **Savannah Energy**, all at early development or announcement stages with limited public detail.

The **Gabès green ammonia pilot** — 220 tonnes H₂ per year and 630 tonnes ammonia per year from 8 MW solar PV — is the most concrete near-term project, included in the national roadmap and supported by KfW funding. However, it faces significant **social licence challenges**: over 40,000 residents of Gabès protested in October 2025 against existing GCT pollution, and the government's controversial decision to reclassify phosphogypsum as non-hazardous in March 2025 intensified local opposition. An **Enel/Eni pilot** near the Transmed pipeline entry point at Cap Bon is at an even earlier stage.

**Verbund** (Austria) is notably the most cross-cutting European partner, appearing in three separate consortia. Combined announced capacity across all MoU-stage projects exceeds **2 million tonnes per year** — against a country that currently generates only 6% of electricity from renewables and has zero electrolyser capacity. This pattern is characteristic of a "land rush" MoU phase rather than an implementation phase.

## Renewables, electricity system, and hydrogen linkages

The hydrogen strategy's viability depends entirely on a renewable energy buildout of unprecedented scale — and Tunisia's track record here is deeply unconvincing. As of mid-2025, installed renewable capacity stands at approximately **787 MW** (485 MW solar, 240 MW wind, 62 MW hydro), representing just **6% of electricity generation**. The country added roughly 474 MW of renewables over nine years (2016–2025), averaging about 53 MW per year. To reach the hydrogen strategy's requirements, Tunisia would need to deploy renewables at roughly **1,000 times** the historical pace.

Even the non-hydrogen renewable targets appear challenging. Reaching **4,850 MW by 2030** requires adding over 4,000 MW in approximately five years — an eightfold acceleration. Recent progress is encouraging but insufficient: AMEA Power's **120 MW Kairouan solar plant** (commissioned December 2025) was Tunisia's first project exceeding 100 MW and the first concession-regime project to reach operation. A World Bank TEREG programme ($430 million, approved November 2025) targets 2.8 GW of new solar and wind by 2028. In December 2025, Tunisia approved tenders for **2 GW wind and 350 MW solar** for 2026.

Tunisia's **solar resource is excellent**: southern regions receive up to 2,600 kWh/m²/year of global horizontal irradiance and over 2,800 kWh/m²/year of direct normal irradiance. The technical renewable potential has been estimated at over 300 GW. The resource is not the constraint — deployment capacity, grid infrastructure, and institutional factors are.

The electricity grid, operated by **STEG**, already runs at near-capacity with no surplus generation. Peak summer demand causes brownouts. The hydrogen strategy explicitly envisions **dedicated, off-grid renewable installations** for electrolysis rather than grid-sourced electricity — a crucial design choice that avoids competing with domestic supply but requires entirely separate infrastructure. UNIDO has warned that "green hydrogen production should not absorb renewable electricity capacities, thereby decelerating domestic energy transition."

The **ELMED interconnector** — a 600 MW HVDC submarine cable between Tunisia and Sicily, developed by STEG and Italy's Terna with ~€920 million in investment — is expected by 2030–2031. While it carries electricity rather than hydrogen, it strengthens the broader Mediterranean energy integration framework. On the hydrogen side, the SoutH2 Corridor received EU Project of Mutual Interest (PMI) status for its offshore section in December 2025.

## Water constraints and environmental considerations

Tunisia is one of the world's most water-scarce countries, with per capita freshwater availability of **377–460 m³ per year** — well below the absolute scarcity threshold of 500 m³. The country's water stress coefficient stands at **96%**, meaning virtually all renewable water resources are already consumed. Five consecutive years of drought through 2024 left dam reservoirs at approximately 35% capacity. Water rationing, including nighttime supply cuts, has been in effect since 2023.

The hydrogen strategy acknowledges this constraint and mandates that **all water for electrolysis must come from seawater desalination** — no freshwater diversion is permitted. Full-scale production by 2050 would require **160–248 million m³ per year** of desalinated water (estimates vary across sources), equivalent to the annual consumption of approximately 5 million Tunisians, or about half the population.

Tunisia currently operates **16 desalination plants** providing roughly 6% of potable water (~39 million m³/year). The government targets **30% of water needs from desalination by 2030**, with major new investments including the Zarat plant at Gabès (expanding to 100,000 m³/day by 2027) and a **$332.5 million World Bank Water Security and Resilience Programme** approved in 2026. However, these desalination plans serve domestic water needs, not hydrogen production.

The strategy proposes that hydrogen-linked desalination plants could **co-produce drinking water** for local communities, creating a potential synergy. But no concrete commitments exist, and the circular dependency is apparent: renewable energy is needed both for hydrogen electrolysis and for powering the desalination plants that supply water for electrolysis. The additional cost of desalination to hydrogen production is modest (approximately **$0.01–0.02 per kg H₂**), but the environmental implications of large-scale brine discharge along Tunisia's Mediterranean coast remain unaddressed in official documents.

## Export strategy, infrastructure, and geopolitics

The **SoutH2 Corridor** is the centrepiece of Tunisia's hydrogen export infrastructure. This 3,300 km pipeline would connect North African production sites through Italy, Austria, and into Germany, with a planned capacity of **4 million tonnes of hydrogen per year** (approximately 163 TWh). Over 65% of the route would repurpose existing gas infrastructure, including the Trans-Mediterranean pipeline that already traverses Tunisia. The corridor consortium comprises Snam (Italy), TAG and GCA (Austria), bayernets (Germany), and SeaCorridor (the Eni/Snam joint venture managing the Trans-Tunisian and Trans-Mediterranean pipelines).

A **Ministerial Joint Declaration of Intent** was signed on 25 January 2025 by Germany, Italy, Austria, Tunisia, and Algeria. The offshore North Africa Hydrogen Backbone section received EU **Project of Mutual Interest (PMI)** status on 1 December 2025. Italian Prime Minister Meloni has described the corridor as "strategic for European security." These are significant political signals — but the pipeline's target operational date of approximately 2030 is widely considered unrealistic. Adrian Odenweller of the Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research has stated plainly: "I do not expect to see any hydrogen imports via the SoutH2 Corridor by the year 2030."

**Germany is Tunisia's primary bilateral hydrogen partner**, having initiated the relationship with the December 2020 "Tunisian-German Alliance for Green Hydrogen" (€31 million, including €25 million from KfW). GIZ developed the national strategy. Italy plays the critical infrastructure role through Snam, Eni, the ELMED interconnector, and the broader Mattei Plan for Mediterranean energy cooperation. The Algeria-Tunisia-Italy energy axis, described by Carnegie as "tenuous but largely effective," is reinforced by existing gas transit relationships.

Compared to North African competitors, **Tunisia ranks third** in hydrogen positioning:

- **Morocco** leads with the most advanced strategic framework (national roadmap since 2020, "Offre du Maroc" programme, EU Green Partnership, and the separate H2Med pipeline to Spain), the strongest renewable deployment, and the lowest perceived investment risk.
- **Egypt** has the largest project pipeline (35+ announced projects), won the first H2Global auction (Fertiglobe), secured a dedicated EU hydrogen MoU at COP27, and attracted €40 billion in investment commitments at its 2024 EU-Egypt Investment Conference. It also has existing LNG and ammonia export infrastructure.
- **Tunisia** has geographic proximity (the shortest Mediterranean crossing to Europe), existing pipeline transit infrastructure, and a published strategy — but lacks a standalone EU hydrogen MoU, has not participated in H2Global auctions, and faces more severe economic and institutional constraints.
- **Algeria** has vast resource potential but has been slow to act, prioritising fossil fuel exports and only recently publishing its hydrogen strategy.

Tunisia has **not yet benefited from European Hydrogen Bank funding** or won any H2Global tenders. The second H2Global auction round (approved December 2024) will include regional lots for Africa — potentially relevant, but competitive.

## Political economy constraints and implementation risks

The constraints facing Tunisia's hydrogen ambitions are structural, not merely technical. They operate at multiple levels simultaneously.

**Macroeconomic fragility** is perhaps the most binding constraint. Tunisia's GDP growth hovered around 1.2–1.4% in 2024, with public debt exceeding **80% of GDP**. Credit ratings are deeply sub-investment grade: **Fitch CCC+**, **Moody's Caa2**. President Kais Saied rejected the $1.9 billion IMF programme in 2022 (never disbursed) and reportedly severed ties with the Fund entirely by 2025, while the Central Bank has been directed to monetise government deficits (up to TND 7 billion at zero interest). This means the **cost of capital for any infrastructure project in Tunisia is extremely high**, and the country cannot mobilise the concessional financing at scale that the hydrogen strategy explicitly requires. The GIZ's own Power-to-X study modelled financing costs as a "primary determinant" of hydrogen production costs and demonstrated that without public de-risking measures, Tunisian green hydrogen would not be competitive.

**Regulatory absence** is the most immediate institutional gap. Tunisia has **no hydrogen-specific legislation**: no legal definition of green hydrogen, no authorisation procedures for production projects, no regulation for hydrogen injection into the gas network, no guarantees-of-origin system, no dedicated fiscal framework, and no special economic zones for hydrogen. The strategy proposes six specific regulatory actions — including amending the 1964 gas decree and STEG's founding statute — but none appear to have been enacted as of early 2026. No hydrogen code comparable to Mauritania's (adopted October 2024) exists.

**Social opposition** has emerged as a serious factor, particularly in Gabès. The city hosts GCT's chemical complex, which has caused decades of severe environmental damage (air pollution, phosphogypsum waste, marine contamination). The **Stop Pollution movement**, founded in 2012, demands dismantling of GCT facilities — not new green hydrogen installations at the same site. Over **40,000 residents** protested in October 2025, and a general strike drew 130,000 participants. A coalition of **87 civil society organisations** signed a statement opposing the SoutH2 Corridor in March 2025. The government's reclassification of phosphogypsum as non-hazardous intensified opposition. These dynamics directly threaten the social licence for the Gabès pilot, the most concrete project in the pipeline.

**STEG's financial distress** — the utility recovers only ~60% of costs, with ~€1.2 billion in accumulated debt and 40% of tariffs covered by state subsidies — constrains grid investment and creates institutional resistance to reforms that might facilitate private sector hydrogen development.

The **principal challenge** is not any single factor but the compounding of constraints: the enormous capital required cannot be raised at Tunisia's credit rating; the regulatory framework needed to attract investors does not exist; the renewable energy buildout needed has not occurred; and the political authority to coordinate across ministries, SOEs, and communities is fragmented. The European Council on Foreign Relations noted in February 2025 that "progress on clean energy cooperation has frequently stalled due to high-level tensions, Tunisia's unpredictable investment environment, and the often-conflicting goals of the many actors on both sides."

## Credibility assessment and outlook

Tunisia's hydrogen sector exists primarily at the level of **narrative and early institution-building**, not bankable implementation. A clear-eyed assessment reveals four distinct layers:

**Narrative and positioning** is well-developed. Tunisia has a published strategy, a ministerial steering committee, international partnerships, and a compelling geographic story. This layer is real and politically significant — it has attracted major corporate attention and European diplomatic engagement.

**Institution-building** is underway but incomplete. The COPIL, the task force, the planned National Observatory, and IRENA's quality infrastructure roadmapping represent genuine institutional scaffolding. But the absence of hydrogen-specific legislation means the institutional framework lacks the legal foundations needed for project development.

**Project preparation** is embryonic. Multiple MoUs exist, but none has progressed to engineering, procurement, and construction. The combined announced capacity exceeds 2 million tonnes per year, but this reflects corporate optionality and political signalling rather than committed investment. The Gabès pilot (220 t/yr) and Enel/Eni pilot are the only projects with near-term plausibility, both at very small scale.

**Bankable implementation** is effectively zero. No FID, no construction, no operational capacity.

**Is Tunisia a serious hydrogen player in North Africa?** It is serious in intent but not yet in capability. It ranks behind Morocco (which has stronger institutions, faster renewable deployment, and lower investment risk) and Egypt (which has a larger project pipeline, existing export infrastructure, and has already won international hydrogen auctions). Tunisia's advantages — proximity to Europe, existing pipeline transit infrastructure, and strong solar resources — are genuine but insufficient without the capital, regulation, and institutional capacity to translate them into projects.

**What is realistic over the next 5–10 years?** The most credible near-term pathway (2026–2030) is the **Gabès green ammonia pilot**, which addresses a genuine industrial need (replacing imported ammonia for GCT's fertiliser operations) and is supported by KfW funding — though social opposition creates significant execution risk. Small-scale pilots with Italian partners near existing pipeline infrastructure are also plausible. Large-scale export production **before the mid-2030s is implausible** based on current trajectory, expert consensus, and the absence of FIDs. If the SoutH2 Corridor progresses and Tunisia can resolve regulatory, financing, and social barriers, initial export-scale projects (in the range of tens of thousands of tonnes per year) might materialise in the **2033–2036 window** — but this requires breakthroughs on multiple fronts simultaneously.

The strategy's 2030 target of 320,000 tonnes — including 300,000 tonnes for export — would require, within approximately four years, building several gigawatts of dedicated renewables, gigawatt-scale electrolysis, desalination capacity, and connecting to a pipeline that does not yet exist, all in a country that has added roughly 50 MW of renewables per year historically. **This target is not credible.**
