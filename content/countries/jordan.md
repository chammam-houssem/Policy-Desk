## mix

Jordan's energy mix is moving to a hybrid structure where gas still leads but renewables now reaching ~27% of electricity generation by 2024 from near zero in 2014. The post-2011 supply crisis was the decisive turning point, triggering LNG imports, long-term gas contracts, and the renewable procurement framework. Policy now favors more renewables, but fossil lock-in and overcapacity still shape dispatch.

**System profile:** Fossil-heavy with gradual diversification

- Gas remains the largest source of electricity
- Renewables rose from near zero in 2014 to roughly one-quarter of generation by 2024
- Oil shale became material after Attarat entered service

**Main driver:** energy security

**Trajectory:** Jordan is likely to add renewables, but gas and oil shale will continue to shape dispatch while contracts persist.

**Sources:** [IEA](https://www.iea.org/countries/jordan/electricity); [Revised/Updated NDC of Jordan](https://www.iea.org/policies/18604-revisedupdated-ndc-of-jordan)

## solar-wind

Solar and wind now play a meaningful role, with solar clearly the larger technology. Deployment accelerated through utility-scale tenders and distributed PV, then slowed after the 2019 freeze before reopening in 2024. The main constraint is the grid and contract structure's ability to absorb more variable generation.

**Status:** Policy ambition ahead of reality

- Renewables reached about 24-27% of generation by 2024
- Distributed solar exceeded 1 GW by end-2024
- No new wind farm was commissioned after 2021

**Constraint:** grid integration

**Implication:** Further solar and wind growth is possible, but storage, interconnections, and fossil contract management will determine system impact.

**Sources:** [IEA](https://www.iea.org/countries/jordan/electricity); [Revised/Updated NDC of Jordan](https://www.iea.org/policies/18604-revisedupdated-ndc-of-jordan)

## hydrogen

Hydrogen is framed as a new export platform for Jordan, closely tied to its renewable buildout and water strategy. The strategy and laws are moving, and the Aqaba hub plus 14 MoUs show real momentum, but deployment is still early and expensive. Desalination makes water more manageable, so infrastructure remains the tighter bottleneck.

**Status:** Project pipeline

- National green hydrogen strategy and new gas/electricity rules
- 14 MoUs and one FEED-stage project
- Planned Aqaba hub linked to desalination and renewables

**Constraint:** infrastructure

**Implication:** Jordan is laying the groundwork for export-scale hydrogen, but near-term output will likely stay at pilot or early hub scale.

**Sources:** [IEA strategy](https://www.iea.org/policies/28776-national-green-hydrogen-strategy); [MEMR draft strategy](https://www.memr.gov.jo/EBV4.0/Root_Storage/AR/EB_Info_Page/GH2_Strategy.pdf); [AAWDCP](https://www.aiib.org/en/projects/details/2025/approved/jordan-aqaba-amman-water-desalination-and-conveyance-project-aawdcp.html)
