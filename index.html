Research

Water-Energy Nexus

Energy Transition under Water Scarcity

My current research examines how water scarcity shapes energy transition outcomes in resource-constrained settings. While water constraints can create strong incentives to accelerate the shift toward renewables, Tunisia presents a case where these incentives have failed to translate into coordinated action. Using the IAD-NAAS framework, I investigate cross-sectoral governance failures between water and energy institutions, drawing on fieldwork and institutional analysis to explain persistent silos and asymmetries.

Authoritarianism & Repression

State Violence and Regime Durability

I have studied authoritarianism across two research collaborations. At Carleton College, under Dr. Kent Freeze, I investigated how different authoritarian regime types employ state repression and how these strategies affect regime survival. I built a dataset capturing all authoritarian regimes since 1992 and their repression patterns. Later, under Dr. Maria Josua at the GIGA Institute, I examined how repression was publicly justified in Ben Ali's Tunisia, analyzing rhetorical legitimation strategies and institutional narratives that normalized state violence.

Debt, Austerity & Fiscal Policy

Fiscal Reform and Social Resilience

At the Institute for Policy Studies, I contributed to the National Priorities Project, analyzing the U.S. federal budget with a focus on military spending and COVID-19 relief. In Tunisia, I led Al Bawsala's research on debt sustainability and fiscal reform, focusing on how austerity policies impacted public services. My work included a detailed analysis of the healthcare system's structural weaknesses and how long-term underinvestment left it vulnerable during the COVID-19 crisis.

Media Ownership & Electoral Coverage

Media Consolidation and Democratic Discourse

At Carleton College, I proposed and conducted a research project under Dr. Barbara Allen examining how corporate media acquisitions influenced electoral coverage. By comparing reporting patterns from the 2016 and 2020 U.S. elections, I analyzed shifts in tone, topic emphasis, and political framing in outlets that underwent ownership changes in the intervening years.
